W 101

Why ResLife welcomes

An introduction to what gives our work meaning — the North Star, mission, vision, RAISE values, and the logic that welcoming staff well is what makes the resident experience possible.

Learning objectives
  1. Explain the North Star, mission, vision, and values that ground Residential Life's approach to community.
  2. Describe how the three pillars — Welcomes, Connects, and Responds — shape the staff role and the resident experience.
  3. Apply the department's priorities and commitments when navigating decisions and interactions.

Our North Star — Building connections. Creating communities.

Community doesn't happen by accident. It is created through intentional connection. Our North Star — Building Connections. Creating Communities. — is the shared approach that brings our mission to life every day. It's not a tagline to memorize. It's a lens to make decisions through.

When staff members ask themselves questions like: should I follow up with this resident? Should I host this program? Should I introduce these two students? — the North Star helps answer the deeper question.

Does this help build connection and create community?If the answer is yes, you are moving in the right direction.

Our mission and vision

Mission

What we do every day

We lead residential communities where every resident feels welcomed, connected, and supported.

Every interaction contributes to whether residents feel welcomed, connected, and supported. These experiences are created through both planned programs and everyday moments.

Vision

What we are working toward

We empower every student to thrive — personally, academically, and beyond UCLA.

Belonging is the foundation. Thriving is the goal. The mission shapes the daily; the vision points to the long arc.

Research consistently shows that students who feel a sense of belonging are more likely to engage with their community, perform better academically, seek help when needed, and persist through challenges.

"Students are more likely to persist when they are academically and socially integrated into the institution."

Vincent Tinto  ·  Leaving College

Residential Life contributes to this by creating environments where belonging can develop.

The three pillars of Residential Life

Residential Life organizes its work around three pillars that shape the resident experience. Each pillar represents a different way staff support residents and communities.

W

ResLife Welcomes

How belonging begins. Welcoming focuses on creating an environment where residents feel included from the moment they arrive and throughout their time with us. Residents are asking: "Do I feel welcome here?"

C

ResLife Connects

How belonging deepens. Connection focuses on building relationships between residents — through programs, conversations, and shared experiences. Residents are asking: "Do I feel connected to people here?"

R

ResLife Responds

How belonging is sustained. Responding focuses on how residents receive care and support — through crisis response, policy enforcement, and connection to resources. Residents are asking: "If I need help, will someone be there?"

Together, these pillars support the full residential experience.

Our priorities — where we focus our energy

Residential Life focuses its work around three priorities that guide where we invest time and resources. They are decisions about what gets our attention, our energy, and our investment.

Wellness First

Sustainable communities require sustainable staff. We prioritize mental, emotional, and physical wellbeing so our teams can show up with care, clarity, and resilience. This is not just a benefit — it is a departmental commitment.

  • Supervisors check in on their staff, not just on their tasks.
  • Staff are encouraged to set boundaries and rest without guilt.
  • When someone is struggling, the team responds.
  • Burnout is treated as a systems problem, not a personal failure.

Psychological safety — the belief that you can speak up, make mistakes, and be honest without fear of punishment — is the foundation of a team that practices Wellness First.

Strength in Partners

Complex challenges require shared ownership. No unit, no floor, no single staff member can deliver the full residential experience alone. We strengthen collaboration with campus partners, faculty, and students to increase impact and reduce isolation.

  • Knowing who your campus partners are before you need them.
  • Connecting residents to resources rather than trying to solve everything yourself.
  • Showing up to cross-unit initiatives with energy, not obligation.
  • Building relationships with colleagues across departments, not just within your team.

Innovation with Insight

We pair creativity with reflection and lived experience — testing ideas thoughtfully, learning quickly, and improving with purpose.

  • Using data from ResLife Pulse, RYE, and program feedback to inform decisions.
  • Being willing to try something new — and honest when it doesn't work.
  • Treating every debrief as a learning opportunity, not a performance review.
  • Asking "how could we do this better?" as a regular practice, not a crisis response.

Our commitments — how we show up

Priorities guide where we invest. Commitments guide how we behave — every day, in every interaction, with every person.

Commitment 01

Practice kindness

Kindness means approaching people with dignity, empathy, and patience — especially during moments of stress or conflict. It applies to residents, to colleagues, and to yourself.

Commitment 02

Focus on your sphere of influence

Redirect your energy toward what you can actually influence — the quality of a conversation, how prepared you are for a shift, whether a colleague feels supported, the tone you set on your floor.

Commitment 03

Be part of the solution

Approach difficulty with curiosity and responsibility rather than blame or disengagement. The question is not "whose fault is this?" The question is: "How can I contribute to making this better?"

Commitment 04

Why tomorrow? Start now.

Progress rarely happens by waiting for perfect conditions. Thoughtful, timely action — even when imperfect — is almost always better than waiting.

Closing synthesis

The Residential Life framework

Residential Life is about more than managing buildings. It is about creating communities where students can live, learn, and grow.

Our North Star calls us to build connections and create communities. Our mission grounds the daily work; our vision points to the long arc. Our values — Respect, Accountability, Integrity, Service, Excellence — guide how we act. Our three pillars — Welcomes, Connects, Responds — organize the work. Our priorities direct our energy. Our commitments shape every interaction.

Belonging is the precondition. Without it, students don't stay, don't reach for help when they need it, don't learn the way they could. Everything else this department does for students depends on it.

Welcoming is not soft work. It's the work that makes the rest possible.

The PRO model — how units organize their work

Every unit at ResLife organizes its work using the same three-part model that anchors how we develop our staff: Philosophy, Roles, Operations. Whether you are looking at Residential Education, Finance, Learning Centers, or any other unit, the same lens applies — and it is the same lens that structures your training. Becoming a PRO at your unit's work means understanding all three.

P

Philosophy

Why the unit exists. What problem it solves. What outcomes it owns. The values and principles that shape how the unit makes decisions and where it draws the line. Philosophy keeps a unit's work tethered to impact, not activity.

R

Roles

Who does the work and how authority and responsibility are distributed. Who leads the unit, what positions exist, how supervision flows, where decisions live. Understanding a unit's roles tells you who to contact, who to collaborate with, and how to escalate.

O

Operations

How the work actually happens, day-to-day. The systems, processes, deliverables, rhythms, and data that turn philosophy and roles into results — programs, services, reporting cycles, dashboards, feedback loops. Operations is also where patterns surface.

When you understand a unit's PRO, you can read it like an operating system.You see what the work is for, who does it, and how it actually moves.

Committees — work across boundaries

Some work benefits the entire department, not just one unit. That is what committees are for. Where units own their individual work, committees own what belongs to everyone.

Each committee is led by a specific unit, supports one or more of the three pillars, and produces deliverables designed for the whole department. Committee chairs and advisors meet quarterly as the Committee of Committees to align on shared direction.

Committee Focus

Committee participation is optional. Joining one is an opportunity to build skills outside your primary role, learn from colleagues across units, and shape department-wide practice. If any of these areas connect to something you care about, committees are where you can do that work.

Closing synthesis

One system, many parts, designed to work together

ResLife is one integrated system delivered through ten units, organized by the same three-part PRO model, connected by committees that do work no single unit owns.

The operating model follows a clear line: mission to pillars to units to committees to meetings to decisions. Your daily work fits somewhere in that flow, whether you are planning a program, responding to an incident, or meeting with your supervisor.

Understanding this structure means you can show up with clarity. You know who to collaborate with. You know how decisions move. You know that the system is designed to support your work, not make it harder.

W 103

Knowing our students

A research-grounded and empathy-driven look at who UCLA's residential community members actually are — their scale, demographics, lived experiences, transition challenges, and what the belonging literature tells us staff need to know before they can truly welcome anyone.

Learning objectives
  1. Describe the scale, selectivity, and demographic composition of UCLA's undergraduate and graduate student populations, and explain what those realities mean for how staff show up.
  2. Identify the lived-experience patterns — first-generation status, financial need, cultural background, identity, international adjustment, family obligations, mental health — that shape how residents experience belonging.
  3. Apply a student-centered lens when designing interactions, programs, and community outreach, informed by who the data says is actually living in your community.

We cannot welcome someone we do not understand. We cannot connect with someone whose story we have never considered. This module is not a demographic report. It is preparation for the specific human beings who will live on your floor, knock on your door, raise their kids next door, and decide — in those first weeks — whether they belong here.

Residential Life at UCLA serves more than a traditional dormitory population. We are responsible for undergraduates, graduate students, postdoctoral scholars, partners, and children — people at every stage of early adulthood. These are the people who chose UCLA over every other option, and we are the first institutional community that greets them.

The scale of who chooses UCLA

UCLA is the most applied-to university in the United States. The students, scholars, and trainees who live in our residential communities represent the small fraction who were offered a place and said yes.

Undergraduates

For Fall 2025, the campus received 173,573 undergraduate applications — 145,086 from prospective first-years and 28,487 from prospective transfers. The first-year who knocks on your door at move-in was admitted at a 9.4% rate and arrived with an average unweighted GPA of 3.94. 58.8% of the enrolled first-year class earned a 4.0 in high school; 93.4% earned a 3.75 or higher.

The transfer student down the hall came from one of the most competitive transfer pools in the country — admit rate 23.4% — with an average GPA above 3.5 and most or all major prep complete. 93% of admitted transfers came from a California community college.

Graduate students

At the graduate level, UCLA received 39,741 applications for Fall 2025 across academic master's and doctoral programs — and this number excludes the four largest professional schools (Dentistry, Law, LLM, and Medicine). Of those applications, the Graduate Division admitted 9,090 students at a 23% admit rate, and 4,188 submitted intent to register.

54% of Fall 2025 grad applicants were international, 32% were Domestic Non-URM, and 14% were Domestic URM. More than half of the people competing for a spot in UCLA's master's and doctoral programs this year were applying from outside the United States.

Whether the resident in front of you is a first-year, a transfer, a master's student, or a doctoral candidate, they share one thing: they beat long odds to be here.And for many of them, that fact quietly becomes pressure instead of pride.

What this means for how you work. Your job is not to flatter residents for getting in. It is to notice when the pressure of "deserving to be here" shows up as isolation, perfectionism, withdrawal, or silent struggle — and to make your community the place where people don't have to perform. Selectivity got them here. Belonging is what keeps them.

Who is actually here — graduate students and postdocs

ResLife doesn't just serve undergraduates. Graduate students make up roughly 29% of the total campus population — and thousands live in UCLA-operated graduate housing communities like Weyburn Terrace, University Apartments South, University Village, Keystone, and the Margan Apartments.

  • Master's students, not PhDs, are the majority. Of the 13,900 grad students, 50.7% are pursuing a master's degree, 33.8% are doctoral, and 15.5% are in professional programs.
  • Far more international than undergrads. 25% of all UCLA graduate students are international — three times the undergraduate international share.
  • Domestic URM representation is meaningful and growing. 24% of the 2025–26 grad student body — 3,294 students — are Domestic URM.
  • Their residential experience is longer and quieter. Grad residents often stay in the same community for two, three, or five years. The rhythm is long-haul.

And beyond the students: postdocs. UCLA supports 1,203 postdoctoral scholars as of Fall 2024 — trainees who already hold their PhD and are spending an average of 3.0 years in postdoctoral research. 51% are international, and 46% are women — up from 39% a decade ago.

Belonging in a grad community is built through small, consistent acts of respect.A maintenance issue answered on time. A community event that includes partners and kids. A neighbor who nods hello in the hallway year after year.

What students are experiencing right now

Knowing demographics is preparation. Knowing what students are carrying this year is what lets you actually respond.

Reality 01

Mental health is a present concern

Nationally, 37% of college students report moderate-to-severe depressive symptoms. 52% report high levels of loneliness. 11% report suicidal thoughts. These are not edge cases — they are the baseline. The staff member who checks in, who pays attention, who names something before the student has the words to — that person is critical.

Reality 02

Basic needs insecurity is real

42% of UC students experience food insecurity. Many do not know about or feel comfortable using the Economic Crisis Response Team or the Basic Needs hub. Your job is to know where to send someone — quickly and confidently — and to create a community where asking for help does not feel like a failure.

Reality 03

The first six weeks are decisive

Research on belonging and college persistence is consistent: the experiences of the first weeks on campus predict whether students stay. UCLA retained 97% of the Fall 2024 entering undergraduate cohort one year later — the product of thousands of small moments of connection.

Reality 04

Isolation hits grad and international students hardest

Graduate students report comparable or higher rates of anxiety and depression than undergraduates in national surveys, and isolation is particularly pronounced for international grad students in the first year of their program.

The journey to a degree

Belonging is not a one-time event at move-in. It is what sustains someone across years of coursework, life events, and quiet moments of doubt.

UCLA awarded 10,409 bachelor's degrees, 3,832 master's degrees, 781 research doctorates, and 597 professional-practice doctorates in 2024–25. The undergraduate six-year graduation rate for the Fall 2019 cohort was 92.8%.

"The six-year graduation rate for Pell Grant recipients was 89.8% — within five points of the overall rate."

UCLA is one of the very few universities where students from lower-income households graduate at nearly the same rate as students who did not need aid.

First-generation students hold their own at these timelines and beyond — 89% of first-gen freshman entrants graduate within six years, and 91% of first-gen transfers graduate within four years. When you follow up with a first-gen resident instead of assuming they're fine, you are part of the system that keeps those numbers where they are.

Don't measure a resident's journey against an imagined timeline.Welcome people into the journey they are actually having — not the one a brochure described.

The belonging gap

Two staff members can do "the same thing" for every resident in their community and produce very different belonging outcomes.

If your default welcome assumes everyone shares a cultural frame, your welcome reaches some people and misses others — even if your effort is equal. Belonging-centered practice means asking, before you design anything: who might not feel included in this, and what would it take to reach them?

This shows up in small decisions. The time of events. The kinds of food you order. The references you make. Whether you follow up once or twice. Whether you learn names in the first week or let it go until week three.

Know the resources

You don't have to be the expert — you have to know where to send

Mental health: CAPS, RISE. Disability support: Center for Accessible Education. Undocumented students: Bruin Resource Center. First-generation: First To Go. International: Dashew Center. LGBTQ+: LGBTQ Campus Resource Center. Financial/basic needs: Economic Crisis Response Team, Basic Needs hub. Veterans: Veteran Resource Center. Graduate students: Division of Graduate Education, Graduate Student Resource Center, GSA. Postdocs: Office of the Vice Provost for Graduate Education, UC postdoc ombuds.

Closing synthesis

Knowing them is the first act of welcome

The work of welcoming residents begins with knowing them. Every interaction you have in the first weeks is shaped by whether you understand who they are.

For staff in direct contact: these are the moments to read — the undergrad quiet at the floor meeting, the transfer who keeps saying "you all have known each other longer," the master's student attending a program alone because their partner is back home, the doctoral student who has been here five years and still feels invisible.

For staff in operational roles: the same context shapes the experience your work creates — the email a resident gets, the form they fill out, the response time on a request, the policy they navigate.

Staff who know their residents welcome them differently. More specifically. More accurately. More humanly.

W 104

Inclusive excellence framework

A standing department-wide workshop series building inclusive excellence as a living practice — facilitated sessions on equity, identity, belonging, and student success that align all ResLife staff across units.

Learning objectives
  1. Define inclusive excellence and explain how it differs from one-time diversity programming.
  2. Describe the four dimensions of inclusive excellence as practiced at UCLA.
  3. Identify three concrete practices that operationalize inclusive excellence in your role.

What inclusive excellence means

The phrase "inclusive excellence" comes from a specific place. The Association of American Colleges and Universities (AAC&U) introduced it in 2005 as a four-part way of moving diversity work from access numbers to actual learning outcomes. Daniel Solórzano, a UCLA researcher, then named the practice that delivers it day-to-day — microaffirmations: the small, consistent acts of inclusion that, over time, do the actual work of building belonging.

Inclusive excellence is the integration of equity, diversity, and inclusion into the core mission of the institution.Not as an add-on, but as a defining quality of the work itself.

For UCLA Residential Life, inclusive excellence means residential communities where every resident experiences belonging, where every staff member can do their best work, and where the conditions for student success are intentionally designed for the full range of identities, backgrounds, and lived experiences in our halls.

It is not

A program calendar checkbox

  • A one-time training
  • A theme month
  • A program calendar checkbox
  • The responsibility of a single staff member or office
It is

Embedded in every system

  • Embedded in every system, decision, and interaction
  • Owned by every staff member at every level
  • Measured by outcomes, not intentions

The four dimensions of inclusive excellence at UCLA

Building on the AAC&U framework, UCLA articulates inclusive excellence across four interconnected dimensions. Residential Life operationalizes each one.

1

Access & success

Who is in our communities, and are they thriving? This dimension asks us to look at retention, satisfaction, and sense of belonging by identity group — and close gaps where they exist.

2

Climate & intergroup relations

What does it feel like to live and work here? This dimension addresses the day-to-day experience of community: how residents treat each other, how staff respond to bias, and how psychological safety is built.

3

Education & scholarship

What do our staff and residents learn about identity, equity, and difference? This dimension shows up in curriculum, programs, and the conversations we facilitate.

4

Institutional infrastructure

How are our policies, hiring practices, budget allocations, and accountability structures designed? This dimension asks us to audit the systems we operate inside.

From framework to practice in Residential Life

Inclusive excellence becomes real in the choices ResLife staff make every day.

In community building

  • Programs reflect the full range of identities, not just the majority.
  • Floor norms are co-created, not imposed.
  • High-volume traditions are interrogated for who they include and who they leave out.

In staff supervision

  • Feedback addresses both performance and the conditions that shape it.
  • Supervisors name and disrupt patterns that disadvantage particular groups.
  • Hiring processes are structured to surface qualified candidates from underrepresented backgrounds.

In incident response

  • Bias incidents are taken seriously and tracked.
  • Response is consistent across resident identities.
  • Documentation captures impact, not just intent.

In your role

  • You notice who is missing from the room.
  • You take responsibility for the climate of your community.
  • You name what you see, even when it is uncomfortable.

Our commitment & your role

Residential Life's commitment to inclusive excellence is not aspirational — it is operational. It shows up in our hiring, our programming, our incident response, our supervision, and our budget.

Your role is not to be perfect. Your role is to be a practitioner.Someone who is willing to learn, to repair when harm happens, and to keep the work going even when it is hard.

Inclusive excellence is built one decision at a time. Every interaction is an opportunity to reinforce or undermine the community we say we want. You are part of building it.

Closing synthesis

Excellence requires inclusion

Excellence in residential life is not possible without inclusion. A community that works for some is not yet a community that works.

The framework gives you language. The four dimensions give you focus. The practices give you a starting point.

The rest is on us — every day, every decision, every interaction.

Elevating every human interaction

The universal service standard every ResLife staff member shares regardless of role — what it means to elevate every interaction as a daily practice of belonging, with residents, colleagues, and families.

Learning objectives
  1. Describe the ResLife service standard and explain why consistency — not warmth — is its core requirement.
  2. Identify what elevating an ordinary interaction looks like in practice, including with peers and colleagues.
  3. Recognize moments where the service standard breaks down and describe what restoring it requires.

Every ResLife staff member shares one standard regardless of role, unit, or seniority. It is not about being the friendliest person in the building. It is about being reliably present and responsive in every interaction, on every shift, whether things are going well or not.

What the service standard actually requires

Consistency is the requirement, not warmth.A staff member who is excellent on good days but visibly checked out on bad ones is not meeting the standard.

Residents learn to trust staff through consistency — through showing up the same way whether it is the first week of the quarter or the eighth, whether the shift has been easy or hard.

Ordinary moments are the most important ones. Residents do not evaluate their belonging by the quality of big programs. They evaluate it by whether someone made eye contact in the hallway, whether a staff member knew their name in week two, whether they felt like an interruption or a person when they approached a desk or office.

Key concept

Microaffirmations

Daniel Solórzano, a UCLA researcher, calls them microaffirmations — the small, consistent acts of recognition that build belonging over time, often more powerfully than any signature event. That's what the service standard is asking you to do. Notice. Acknowledge. Repeat.

The standard applies to peer interactions too. How you speak to a colleague in front of a resident, how you show up in a team meeting, how you respond when a peer is struggling — all of it is part of the same commitment. A community that feels safe to residents is almost always one where the staff feel safe with each other first.

What breaking down looks like

The service standard does not fail dramatically. It fails in small ordinary moments — and those moments compound.

Scenario 01

At a front desk during move-in

A resident asks where to get a mail key. The staff member is on the phone with three people waiting and points to a sign without looking up. The resident walks away with a first impression they will carry all year.

What elevating it requires: "I'll be with you in just a moment" — then following through. The standard does not require never being busy. It requires staying present even when you are.

Scenario 02

In a debrief after a hard shift

A supervisor is debriefing with a staff member and responding to messages while the staff member talks. The staff member shortens what they were going to say. The debrief that was supposed to provide support provides none.

What elevating it requires: Putting the phone down is not a small gesture — it is the standard.

Scenario 03

In an informal moment

A resident sees a familiar staff member in a public space and starts venting about a difficult situation. The staff member laughs awkwardly. The resident stops and walks away. Two days later, the situation escalates.

What elevating it requires: Find 90 seconds even in an inconvenient moment. The break wasn't the laugh — it was the signal that there was a wrong time to be a resident with a problem.

Scenario 04

Between colleagues

A staff member rolls their eyes when a peer asks a question they consider basic. A resident sees it. The lesson learned: this is what asking for help looks like here.

What elevating it requires: Treating every question — from anyone — like the person asking deserves your full attention.

Staying present when you are stretched

The hardest part of the service standard is meeting it on the bad days — when you are exhausted, overwhelmed, or carrying something personal.

1

Before a shift

Take a minute to transition. Not to feel different, but to make a deliberate choice about presence. What is one specific thing you noticed about your area this week that you can bring into this shift?

2

During a shift

When an interaction catches you off-guard, slow down. "Let me make sure I understand what you're asking" buys you a moment and signals you are taking the person seriously.

3

After a difficult interaction

Name what happened, even just to yourself. "That was hard" or "I handled that poorly" — both are more useful than moving on without processing. Acknowledging keeps it from accumulating into a pattern.

There will be moments you do not meet the standard.The question is whether you notice when that happens and what you do next.

The standard week by week

The service standard is a daily accumulation of small decisions.

Week 1 — Learn the names

Learn the names of the people in your immediate community — colleagues, residents, the regulars who come through your space. Not most of them — every one. This signals that each person is a person, not an occupant.

Week 2 — Notice who is missing

Notice who has been quiet or absent and reach out. Not with a program — with a question: "I haven't seen you around. How are you settling in?" That moment may be the most important thing you do all quarter.

Week 3 — Audit yourself

Pick one interaction from the past week where you were not fully present and think about what got in the way. Understanding what pulls you from the standard is how you improve at meeting it.

Ongoing — Name when it works

When something goes well — a resident genuinely helped, a hard moment handled well — name it. The service standard is sustainable only if it connects to why the work matters.

Closing synthesis

A choice made shift after shift

The service standard is not a performance. It is a choice — to treat every person you encounter as someone who deserves your presence and attention, even in the ordinary moments, even on the hard days.

That choice, made consistently across hundreds of staff, is what makes ResLife feel welcoming to the 26,000+ students we serve this year.

Each one of them is asking, consciously or not, whether they belong here. What you do in the ordinary moments of your job is part of how they answer that question.

Team dynamics & development

Understanding how teams form, navigating group dynamics, building peer accountability, and accelerating cohesion so that department teams function as a unit rather than a collection of individuals.

Learning objectives
  1. Apply Tuckman's stages of team development to your team and identify where your team currently sits in that progression.
  2. Implement peer accountability practices that build trust and accelerate team cohesion.
  3. Navigate team conflict and build intentional cohesion across the year.

In ResLife, teams don't happen by accident. This module focuses on how teams form, how members fit together, and how to intentionally build cohesion so your team functions as a unit rather than a collection of individuals.

How teams form in ResLife

Teams go through predictable stages as they develop. Bruce Tuckman's model identifies four. In ResLife, these stages matter because your team composition changes from year to year. New members join. Others move on. Roles shift.

1

Forming

People first come together — often at summer training. Learning names, figuring out roles. Set expectations, introduce routines, establish psychological safety. The stage feels positive because conflict hasn't surfaced. Don't mistake that for a fully formed team.

2

Storming

Differences surface. Two colleagues disagree. Team members push back. Vision doesn't match execution. It looks like conflict — and it's where teams actually form. The goal is to navigate disagreement productively, not eliminate it.

3

Norming

After working through conflict, the team develops shared norms — how leaders respond under pressure, what standards staff are held to, the inside jokes and shared language, the trust. Built through consistency and clear communication.

4

Performing

The team executes at high capacity. People understand their role and how it connects to others. Conflicts resolve quickly because there's trust. Communication is efficient. The people you serve are better served because the team is aligned.

"Teams perform when their members believe they can speak up, disagree, and admit mistakes without being punished for it."

Amy Edmondson  ·  The Fearless Organization — and Google's Project Aristotle

Teams without psychological safety stall in storming. Teams with it move through. Your job is to move the team through these stages with intention: create safety in forming, address conflict directly in storming, build norms through consistency, and protect performing by holding onto what works.

Peer accountability and trust

Accountability doesn't require formal position. A peer can hold another peer accountable. A team member can name something when a supervisor isn't aligned with what was decided. Peer accountability is actually the fastest way to change behavior because it's immediate and relational.

Trust is built on three things.Consistency. Competence. Care.
C

Consistency

People do what they say they're going to do. You show up on time. You honor commitments even when inconvenient. People in ResLife notice what you do when it's hard, not just when it's easy.

C

Competence

People believe you know what you're doing. Not perfect — but you've thought through your decisions and can execute. When a leader follows through on a commitment to address a problem, the team sees competence.

C

Care

People believe you have their best interest in mind. Hardest to build because it can't be faked. You build it by being genuinely interested in growth, advocating for people, remembering what matters to them, being honest about what you need.

Difficult conversations as team building

Calling someone in about a pattern. Naming tension. Disagreeing with a decision. These conversations are uncomfortable, but they're also where teams actually build trust. When you disagree, listen, stay curious, and come out with greater understanding, you've just proven that conflict doesn't destroy the relationship.

A pattern that works

Name. Ask. Listen. Share. Discuss. Follow up.

"I noticed you've left team meetings early the last three times. What's going on?" The supervisor isn't attacking — they're curious. The team member explains. The supervisor listens, then names the impact: "Those meetings are how we sync. When people leave early, information doesn't spread. What do we need to adjust so this works for you?" Now you're problem-solving, not punishing.

Navigating conflict and tension productively

Not all conflict is the same. Disagreement may be about approach, values, or feeling disrespected — and your response depends on which one is actually happening.

Type 01

Approach disagreements

Usually resolvable. Two team leads disagree about check-in cadence. Different philosophies, both with merit. Discuss trade-offs, gather data, decide. You're not disagreeing about the goal — you're disagreeing about the method.

Type 02

Values disagreements

Trickier. These don't resolve by voting. They resolve by clarity about non-negotiables. Within RAISE, there's room for different approaches. But if someone is violating one of those values, that's not a different approach — that's a problem.

Type 03

Power or respect disagreements

Stickiest because they threaten the relational fabric. If someone feels their input doesn't matter, that changes how they show up. These need to be named directly and addressed with commitment to hearing each person's perspective.

The role of the supervisor in conflict

When conflict emerges, your job is not to eliminate it. Your job is to help the team work through it in a way that strengthens trust rather than eroding it.

  • Bring people together. Ask them to explain perspectives to each other.
  • Ask clarifying questions: "What are you each actually trying to accomplish here?"
  • Sometimes you need to make a call. When you do, explain your reasoning. Acknowledge what each person cared about. Commit to checking in.
  • If you are the one in conflict with a staff member, you have responsibility to address it. You can't outlast someone or assume your position resolves it.

Building cohesion intentionally

Waiting for a team to bond rarely works. Time alone often means conflict gets buried, not resolved. Intentional team building is what accelerates cohesion.

Structure experiences beyond roles

A team lunch isn't frivolous. It's where staff see their supervisor as a person. Where a leader observes how the team moves together. Where people remember that they're human.

Shared language builds identity

When your team develops short-hand or inside references or shared values language, you're building identity. The team becomes "the kind of people who" do things a certain way. That identity then governs behavior.

Rituals create predictability

Your weekly staff meeting. Your end-of-the-year celebration. Your one-on-one check-ins. When people know what to expect and trust that the ritual serves them, they show up fully.

Recognize and celebrate

Cohesion accelerates when people know they're valued. A quick text to a team member who handled a situation well. Calling out a peer's strength in a meeting. Following through on what someone asked for. Without recognition, people do the minimum and check out emotionally.

The timeline

Where teams sit through the year

Summer training is forming. Fall is storming into early norming. By winter, healthy teams sit solidly in norming with moments of performing. Spring is testing what's been built. If your team feels tense in October, that's storming. If it still feels tense in January, that's worth investigating.

Closing synthesis

From individual roles to unified teams

Knowing the org chart is not the same as building a team. The work is moving from a collection of individuals with distinct roles to a unit that actually functions together.

The answer is not revolutionary: clarity about roles, addressing conflict directly, building trust through consistency, competence, and care. Storming is not a failure — it's a stage you move through. Cohesion is built, not inherited.

When your team functions well, the people you serve benefit. They get consistent messages from staff. They see adults working well together. They experience belonging because the team itself is cohesive enough to create it.

Get your team right. Everything else becomes easier.

Emotional intelligence

The four domains of emotional intelligence — self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, and relationship management — and how they show up in supervision, response, and community building when the work gets hard.

Learning objectives
  1. Define emotional intelligence and identify its four core domains.
  2. Recognize how emotional intelligence shows up in residential life work — in supervision, response, and community building.
  3. Apply emotional intelligence practices to challenging interactions with staff and residents.

What emotional intelligence is (and is not)

Emotional intelligence is the ability to recognize emotions in yourself and others, manage your responses, and use that awareness to guide your actions and relationships.

It is not

Performing positivity

  • Suppressing feelings
  • Always being calm
  • Avoiding hard conversations
  • Performing positivity
It is

Choosing your response

  • Knowing what you are feeling and why
  • Choosing your response instead of reacting
  • Reading the emotional reality of the room
  • Building trust over time
In residential life, emotional intelligence is not a soft skill.When you move from a roommate conflict to a programming meeting to a duty call in the span of an hour, EI is the operating system.

The four domains

Daniel Goleman's model organizes emotional intelligence into four domains. Effective ResLife staff develop all four.

1

Self-awareness

Noticing your emotions in real time and understanding what triggers them. Without self-awareness, the other three domains collapse. Name the emotion before reacting. Track your patterns. Ask for feedback on how you show up under stress.

2

Self-management

Regulating your emotional responses and choosing your behavior — especially under pressure. Pause before responding to charged messages. Use breath, movement, or distance to reset. Separate the trigger from the response.

3

Social awareness

Reading the emotional state of others and the dynamics of a group. Notice nonverbal cues — tone, posture, energy. Ask about impact, not just facts. Understand the context behind a behavior before judging it.

4

Relationship management

Building trust, navigating conflict, and influencing outcomes through connection. Repair quickly when you misstep. Give feedback that lands without burning the relationship. Hold both warmth and accountability in the same conversation.

EI in practice

Emotional intelligence is invisible until you watch it in action.

Scenario 01

A resident is upset about a situation

Low EI: Match their energy, defend the policy, escalate to write-up.

High EI: Regulate yourself first. Acknowledge what they are feeling. Get curious about what they actually need. Hold the policy with steadiness.

Scenario 02

A staff member misses a deadline for the third time

Low EI: Frustrated email, public correction, assumption of bad intent.

High EI: Check yourself. Have a direct, private conversation. Ask what is happening. Hold the standard while opening the door to support.

Scenario 03

A peer makes a comment that lands wrong

Low EI: Avoid, gossip, freeze the relationship.

High EI: Name the impact privately. Stay curious about intent. Repair the relationship explicitly.

The pattern

Regulate. Observe. Choose.

Across every scenario, the move is the same. Regulate yourself first. Observe what's actually happening. Then choose your response — instead of letting the moment choose for you.

Building your EI practice

Emotional intelligence develops through small, consistent actions.

D

Daily

Name one emotion during your day. Just one. Out loud or in writing. The act of labeling builds the muscle.

W

Weekly

Ask for one piece of feedback. From a peer, a supervisor, or someone you supervise. Make it specific.

O

Ongoing

Notice your biggest trigger and plan your response ahead of time. Knowing your trigger is half the regulation work.

Growth comes from repetition, not perfection.
Closing synthesis

The skill that holds the rest together

Every other skill in residential life — supervision, response, community building, conflict resolution — depends on emotional intelligence.

This is the skill that determines whether the rest hold up or fall apart.

Dialogue across difference

How to have meaningful conversations across identity and lived experience, navigate tension without shutting it down, and create conditions where residents and staff can actually hear each other.

Learning objectives
  1. Distinguish dialogue from debate and describe the conditions that make dialogue possible.
  2. Use facilitation moves that keep cross-identity conversations productive when tension rises.
  3. Recognize when a conversation needs escalation or referral.

Dialogue across differences is not conflict resolution or debate. It is the practice of creating space for people with different experiences to hear each other — without requiring agreement.

In residential life, these conversations happen constantly: between roommates, across cultures and identities, and within staff teams. The question is not whether they will happen, but whether you are prepared to hold them.

Dialogue vs. debate

Mode 01

Debate

About being right. One person shares a perspective, the other feels challenged, and both shift from listening to defending. The result is deeper entrenchment.

Mode 02

Dialogue

About understanding. The goal is to leave with a clearer view of another person's experience — not to win.

These practices draw from decades of research on intergroup dialogue, including work by Ximena Zúñiga and Biren Nagda at the University of Michigan, showing that structured dialogue shifts how people understand each other.

What makes dialogue possible

1

Genuine curiosity

You have to actually want to understand the other person's experience, not just explain your own. This is harder than it sounds when you feel certain you are right.

2

Tolerance for discomfort

Cross-identity dialogue is often uncomfortable. Sitting with that discomfort without shutting the conversation down is part of the practice.

3

Suspension of conclusions

You hold your own interpretation loosely enough to allow new information in. You do not stop having a perspective — you hold it differently.

Facilitation moves that help

In one-on-one conversations

  • Ask open questions first. "What has that been like for you?" surfaces more than "Did that bother you?" The first creates space for the person to describe their actual experience.
  • Reflect before you respond. "It sounds like you felt unseen in that situation." People who feel heard are much more willing to hear something back.
  • Name the tension without collapsing it. "I hear that we see this differently, and I think both perspectives are worth taking seriously." Collapsing tension often leaves the real issue unaddressed.

In group settings

  • Be intentional about who speaks first. The person who speaks first often sets the frame. If that person reflects only one slice of the room's experience, others may feel they cannot add a different perspective.
  • Watch for silencing dynamics. When certain voices dominate and others go quiet, that's a reflection of who feels safe speaking — not who has more to say.
  • Create multiple on-ramps. Written reflection before group discussion. Pair conversations before full-group sharing. Explicit invitations to perspectives that have not yet been voiced.

When tension rises

Tension is not failure.Difficult conversations across difference often get tense. It is often a sign that something real is being touched.

Your job when tension rises is not to eliminate it. It is to keep it from collapsing into shutdown, personal attack, or harm. There is a difference between productive tension — the kind that comes from genuinely engaging with hard things — and destructive tension, where people are no longer able to hear each other.

A useful word

Microaggressions

Daniel Solórzano at UCLA gave us racial microaggressions — the brief, usually unintentional verbal or behavioral moments that communicate exclusion. When tension rises in a cross-identity conversation, a microaggression has often just landed. Naming what you saw — rather than guessing at intent — is one of the most useful moves in your toolkit.

Moves when tension spikes

  • Slow the pace. Speak more slowly yourself. It often brings the temperature down.
  • Name what you observe, not what you interpret. "I notice we have both gotten more reactive in the last few minutes" is different from "You are being aggressive."
  • Take a break if needed. "I want to keep this going, but I think we both need a few minutes. Can we come back to this in an hour?"

Knowing your limit

Some conversations carry weight that is beyond what a staff member should hold alone. If a conversation involves a serious incident of bias, discrimination, or harm, your role is not to mediate it — it is to connect the person to appropriate resources and escalate to your supervisor or the Civil Rights Office.

Connecting to ResLife values

Value

Respect requires more than politeness

Respect at ResLife means making every person feel valued, heard, and included. In cross-identity dialogue, that requires actually listening — including to things that challenge your assumptions or make you uncomfortable.

Value

Service means showing up for hard conversations

The residents who most need someone to hold a difficult conversation with them are often the most isolated. Service means showing up even when you are not sure you will do it perfectly.

Closing synthesis

One of the most demanding things you will do

Dialogue across differences is one of the most demanding things you will do in residential life — and one of the most important.

The communities where residents develop genuine connection across identity are the ones where someone was willing to hold those conversations with care and skill.

Your effectiveness depends on self-awareness. Build the skill, keep practicing, and take care of your capacity to do this work well.

AI as a teammate

How AI tools can strengthen daily ResLife work, from drafting communications to summarizing student interactions, while keeping human judgment, FERPA compliance, and equity at the center.

Learning objectives
  1. Evaluate where AI tools can support daily ResLife tasks and where human judgment must remain.
  2. Create a draft communication or program plan using a generative AI tool, then revise it to reflect ResLife voice and RAISE values.
  3. Analyze the privacy, equity, and ethical boundaries that govern responsible AI use in a residential setting.

Every tool in your ResLife toolkit exists to help you show up better for the people you serve. AI is the newest one.

It cannot replace the conversation you have with someone who needs you at 11 PM or the judgment call you make in a high-stakes moment. But it can take repetitive work off your plate so you have more time and energy for what actually requires you.

What AI actually does

How it works

Generative AI tools like ChatGPT, Copilot, and Gemini predict the next most likely word based on patterns in the data they were trained on. They are not thinking. They do not understand your residents. They are pattern-matching at scale.

AI can draft a welcome email in seconds because those follow predictable patterns. It cannot determine whether a specific resident needs a softer tone due to a complex family situation. That judgment is yours.

What it is good at

Repetitive, pattern-based, low-stakes

  • Drafting routine communications
  • Brainstorming ideas
  • Summarizing documents or notes
  • Creating first drafts of internal content
  • Generating discussion questions for meetings
What it is bad at

Context, nuance, accuracy

  • Anything requiring local knowledge or emotional nuance
  • Producing factually correct text — it "hallucinates"
  • Remembering your team or last week's events
  • Ethical judgment
  • Anything specific to a real person without exposing their data

"AI should reduce administrative repetition so staff can focus on human connection."

Pete Trentacoste  ·  ACUHO-I Talking Stick — AI as a Housing Partner (Sept 2025)

AI handles the draft; you handle the relationship.

AI in your ResLife work

1

Communications & outreach

Welcome messages, follow-ups, reminders, check-ins. AI generates a strong first draft in seconds. The key is that it is a first draft — you revise for tone, audience, and voice.

2

Programming & brainstorming

Useful when you are stuck. Ask for event ideas, icebreakers, passive engagement concepts. You'll get a mix of strong and generic — that's fine. The value is speed.

3

Summarizing & organizing

Tools like ResLife Pulse and RYE generate large amounts of data. AI can identify themes faster than manual review. Two rules: never include identifiable info, always verify the output.

4

Preparing for hard talks

AI can be a useful rehearsal partner. Describe the situation without real names and have the tool role-play different approaches. It does not replace your supervisor — it helps you organize your thinking before the real conversation.

The boundaries that matter

FERPA comes first

When you type a resident's name, student ID, room number, or details about their conduct history into a generative AI tool, that information leaves UCLA's systems and enters a third-party platform. That is a FERPA concern.

Never input personally identifiable student information into unapproved AI tools.Names tied to academic, conduct, or personal details. Room assignments linked to individuals. Incident reports. Notes from one-on-one conversations.

If using AI for a situation, remove all identifying details. AACRAO's 2025 FERPA guidance reinforces the need for proactive protection when student data intersects with AI.

Equity and bias

AI tools are trained on data that reflects existing inequities. A roommate-matching algorithm trained on historical preferences can replicate segregation patterns. A text generator asked to describe "a typical staff member" may default to assumptions about gender, race, or background that do not reflect your actual team.

NASPA's 2024 report on AI in student affairs flags this directly: AI should be audited for bias before being used in any decision that affects students. For your daily work, read what AI produces with a critical eye. Ask who is represented and who is missing.

When not to use AI

  • Conduct decisions
  • Final incident reports
  • Crisis response
  • Direct communication where human presence is essential
  • Any task requiring protected student data
AI handles routine work. You handle the human work.

Try it yourself

1

Draft a welcome message

Use ChatGPT, Copilot, or Gemini to draft a welcome message. Include context, an event, and tone. Edit until it reflects your voice. Notice what felt generic or off.

2

Brainstorm ideas

Generate eight ideas for an event. Evaluate them against your actual community. Which fit? Which assume the wrong context?

3

Review for bias

Take one AI-generated description and read it through an equity lens. Does the language assume a specific cultural context? Would the activity be accessible to people with different abilities, finances, or comfort levels?

Closing synthesis

A tool, not a replacement

AI is a tool that reduces repetitive work so you can focus on what matters most.

The tools will evolve. The standard will not: every tool should help you be more present for the people you serve.

ResLife's priority of Innovation with Insight applies directly. Test ideas thoughtfully. Learn quickly. Improve with purpose. And Why Tomorrow? Start Now applies too. You do not need to become an AI expert before you try your first prompt.

The teammate is ready when you are.

Supervision

How supervision works at ResLife, how to receive feedback without defensiveness, and how to take ownership of your own professional growth.

Learning objectives
  1. Explain the supervision philosophy that guides ResLife supervisor–supervisee relationships.
  2. Identify the rights and responsibilities of supervisors and supervisees within the department.
  3. Apply structured feedback practices in 1:1s, real-time coaching, and formal evaluations.

Why supervision matters in ResLife

Supervision is the most consequential relationship in a staff member's experience. Gallup's workplace data consistently identifies it as the #1 predictor of engagement and retention, and Winston and Creamer's Supervising Student Affairs Staff (1997) made the same case decades ago.

It shapes how work gets done, how staff grow, how problems surface, and whether people stay.

In residential life, supervisors are not just managers.They are the developmental anchor for student staff and early-career professionals. They set the standard and create the conditions for success.

A weak supervision relationship undermines every system. A strong one sustains staff through the hardest parts of the year.

The ResLife supervision philosophy

Our supervision philosophy is built on three commitments.

1

Care + accountability

Supervisors hold both. Care without accountability becomes permissiveness. Accountability without care becomes coercion. The work is to hold both at the same time.

2

Development over compliance

We supervise to grow people, not to control them. Compliance is the floor, not the ceiling. Development is the goal.

3

Equity in practice

Standards apply consistently. Support is calibrated to need. Equal treatment is not the same as equitable treatment, and our supervisors know the difference.

These commitments are not abstract. They show up in every 1:1, every feedback conversation, every evaluation.

The supervisor–supervisee relationship

The relationship is reciprocal. Both sides have responsibilities.

Supervisor

Responsibilities

Recruiting & selecting staff

How we find and choose the right people — recruiting with intention, designing equitable selection processes, and making values-aligned hiring decisions for both student and professional staff.

  2. Describe what makes a selection process equitable and why structured interviews reduce bias.
  3. Apply values-alignment criteria when evaluating candidates for student staff positions.

Hiring is the first decision in the staff lifecycle. Every person who joins ResLife carries the department's values forward — or doesn't. Getting recruitment and selection right is not just an HR function. It is how culture is built and sustained.

Why we recruit the way we do

ResLife recruits over 800 staff members annually — 100+ professional staff, 700+ student staff, and 18 Faculty-in-Residence. The volume is significant. The stakes are higher.

We prioritize relationship-based recruiting over passive job posting because the people who thrive in ResLife are often not reached through generic listings. They are introduced through relationships — a friend who is an RA, a professor who understands the role, a former resident who experienced its impact. Those connections provide context a job posting cannot.

We are explicit about what we stand for in the hiring process.A person with strong organizational skills but weak accountability is not a fit, regardless of their resume.

The Recruitment & Selection Committee coordinates this work — approving job descriptions, overseeing recruitment campaigns, designing interview protocols, and making recommendations. A formal committee ensures consistency across departments and reduces the risk that hiring becomes overly subjective.

Designing equitable selection

How you assess candidates shapes who you hire. Interviews are not neutral; outcomes vary based on structure, panel composition, and question design.

Data from NACE (National Association of Colleges and Employers) and CUPA-HR (College and University Professional Association for Human Resources) consistently finds that structured selection processes outperform unstructured interviews in predicting job performance.

1

Structured interviews

Ask every candidate the same questions in the same order, scored against the same rubric. This reduces the effect of personality, demographics, and interviewer mood. Unstructured interviews tend to favor candidates who remind interviewers of themselves.

2

Rubrics

Make criteria transparent. A strong answer about resolving conflict might demonstrate curiosity, acknowledge one's own role, and connect to RAISE values. That specificity lets every interviewer apply the same standard.

3

Panel composition

A panel that reflects the diversity of our communities surfaces signals that a homogeneous panel misses. Where possible, include both professional and student staff panelists.

Recruiting student staff

Student staff recruitment runs at high volume and requires a process that maintains rigor without becoming inaccessible. ResLife hires 327+ RAs plus students across nine other functional units.

Stage 01

Outreach

Info sessions, floor visits, peer referrals. Reach students who might not have considered the role without a personal introduction to what it actually involves.

Stage 02

Application screen

Screen for minimum qualifications. Move qualified candidates forward into the next stage of the process.

Stage 03

Group process

Candidates collaborate on tasks or scenarios. Reveals how they engage with peers, navigate disagreement, and respond to feedback. Also provides a realistic preview of the role.

Stage 04

Structured interview

Individual interviews focused on values alignment, leadership experience, and residential scenarios. Technical competence matters. Values alignment matters more.

Closing synthesis

Recruitment is where culture begins

Every hire either strengthens or dilutes what ResLife is. A values-aligned team built through intentional, equitable selection practices will outperform a technically skilled team that lacks coherence — especially in the interpersonal, high-stakes work of residential education.

When you are involved in selection — reviewing applications, sitting on an interview panel, or referring a candidate — you are participating in one of the most consequential things ResLife does. Treat it accordingly.

Developing staff

Staff Development is not a single event. It is a continuous cycle built into how ResLife operates. A person joins as a new hire, attends summer training, receives ongoing development opportunities, grows through feedback and mentorship, and eventually transitions to another role. At each stage, ResLife invests intentionally in building competencies and connecting learning to real work.

The development philosophy

Learning in ResLife is progressive, not episodic. Summer training is the foundation. Developmental training throughout the year deepens skills. Feedback loops reinforce what matters. Movement into new roles creates opportunities to apply what was learned in a previous context.

Staff capability compounds when learning is intentional, connected to actual work, and embedded in daily life.

A new staff member completes summer training with foundational knowledge and applicability to their work for the coming year. They understand our philosophy, know their core roles & responsibilities, and have envisioned practical examples for application. Team members should be able to assess their community, navigate conflict when it arises, and show genuine care when serving someone who is struggling.

The Development Committee drives this work — examining feedback, identifying gaps, designing new learning experiences, and monitoring whether development actually shifts how staff work. Training Assistants are the operational backbone, providing the vision for training 500+ student leaders across the department.

The training architecture

ResLife uses a three-part training structure.

1

Summer training

ProStaff Training (late July–mid-August) and Student Staff Training (late August–September). Multi-week training combining Centralized Departmental Training and individual Team/Unit Sessions. Most include a Team Training & Development Retreat.

2

Quarterly in-service

Three times per year (Fall, Winter, Spring), typically 2–3 hours. Topics driven by feedback from staff, the Development Committee, and assessment data. Addresses what emerged throughout the year and deepens skills that need reinforcement.

3

Individual development

1:1 conversations with supervisors, mentorship, optional workshops, and self-directed learning. The Development Committee maintains a catalog of optional learning opportunities for staff interested in stretching into new areas.

The PRO model

Philosophy. Roles. Operations.

Philosophy (why we do this work) is first, because staff make better decisions when they understand our shared values. Roles (what each position is accountable for) is next. Operations (how we execute daily) follows, providing the systematic execution that ensures consistency.

The LED Method

Learn. Engage. Deepen.

Within each PRO section, the LED Method structures the learning: Learn (new information), Engage (applied practice), Deepen (synthesis and reflection).

Learning in the flow of work

The most important learning happens in our work. Growth is continuous for both Professional and Student Staff Members. Here are a few ways continuous development shows up in UCLA ResLife.

Mode 01

Supervision as development

1:1s are not status meetings — they are development conversations. A supervisor asks: What did those individuals need? How did you respond? What would you do differently? This kind of questioning builds judgment over time by connecting abstract frameworks to concrete decisions.

Mode 02

Structured reflection

During project/committee debriefs and post-program assessments — move-in, a community crisis, a program — ask everyone to reflect. What went well? What would they do differently? What did they learn? This turns experience into competency.

Mode 03

Weekly / quarterly team meetings

Reflection within our team is essential. In Residential Education, RAs meet weekly on Monday nights to combine training on a current topic with community-building and operational updates. Other units have their own rhythms — but the principle is the same.

The principle

Development happens through consistent rhythms

Not just summer training. Whatever the cadence, staff leave with both knowledge and the sense that their supervisor understands what they are working with week to week.

Closing synthesis

How we honor the time and care staff give

Development is foundational in how ResLife honors the time and care that staff give to residents and one another.

The structures are in place: summer training, in-service, feedback loops, mentorship, and individual development opportunities.

What brings them to life is execution — supervisors asking the reflective questions, trainers designing engaging experiences, staff showing up ready to learn.

Offboarding staff

How we honor what staff built and prepare for what comes next — documenting institutional knowledge, creating clean handoffs, and ensuring that every departure strengthens the people and systems that follow.

Learning objectives
  1. Explain what knowledge loss costs a residential community and why offboarding is a professional responsibility, not a formality.
  2. Describe the components of an effective student staff offboarding process.
  3. Apply key practices from professional staff transition planning to ensure continuity when someone leaves.

Offboarding is not an exit. It is a bridge between who your team was and who it will become. When a staff member leaves, they carry with them relationships, expertise, and institutional memory that are difficult to rebuild. How we manage departures signals what we value — and shapes the experience of everyone who stays.

What is at stake when someone leaves

Every staff member holds information that exists nowhere else: how a particular resident responds to conflict, which events work best in a given community, what happened during a crisis last fall, why a certain decision was made. Much of this knowledge is implicit — it lives in memory, in judgment, in the patterns someone built through months of experience.

When that person leaves without a structured offboarding process, the knowledge leaves with them.

This loss affects the community directly. Incoming staff rebuild relationships from scratch. The next professional staff hire recreates context that their predecessor simply knew. The people we serve lose a trusted point of contact. Operations slow down because the incoming person has to learn through trial and error what their predecessor understood through experience.

The morale cost is also real. When a staff member who has poured energy into the community leaves without acknowledgment, the message to everyone remaining is clear: contributions are fungible, people are replaceable. Thoughtful offboarding sends the opposite message — that the time and care someone gave here mattered.

Student staff offboarding

Student staff transitions are predictable and occur in volume. The end-of-year checkout is the opportunity to capture what they learned and honor what they contributed.

1

Exit conversations

Substantive, not a form. Ask: What situation required the most problem-solving from you? What decision by leadership felt misaligned with values? What should we teach new staff that we currently don't? These questions surface recurring bottlenecks and training gaps.

2

Documentation handoffs

Capture role-specific information: contact lists, ongoing situations, community context, plus operational knowledge about how certain systems or processes actually work. Where possible, pair incoming staff with outgoing staff for a few hours.

3

Recognition & closure

A dinner, a recognition event, a handwritten note, a moment in a staff meeting. The form matters less than the signal: we are pausing to acknowledge that your time here mattered.

Professional staff transitions

Professional staff departures carry higher stakes and require more extensive preparation. Effective transitions begin months before departure.

Phase 01

Transition planning

Identify what must be transferred: files, contact lists, project status, recurring meetings, decision frameworks, key relationships. Also identify what must be created — documentation that did not previously exist.

What community actually is

Every year, more than 26,000 residents live in our residential communities. They are spread across the Hill, University Apartments North, and University Apartments South. Across all of those communities, thousands of programs happen. Thousands of conversations take place in hallways, lounges, and shared spaces. And more than 800 professional and student staff members and faculty-in-residence show up every day to help build and sustain that community.

But here is what those numbers do not tell you.

Not every resident who lives in one of our buildings actually feels like they belong to it.

Community is not measured by square footage. It is not measured by headcount. It is not measured by the number of events on a calendar. It is measured by whether residents actually feel seen, feel that they matter, and feel like they are part of something real.

"I am seen here. I matter here. I am part of this place."That feeling is what we call belonging. And belonging is what community makes possible.

Research consistently backs this up. Students who feel like they belong are more likely to stick with college academically. They are more likely to ask for help when they need it. They build stronger peer support networks. And they experience lower levels of distress and isolation.

Terrell Strayhorn, one of the leading researchers on belonging, puts it this way:

"A sense of belonging is a basic human need and motivation, sufficient to influence behavior."

Terrell Strayhorn  ·  College Students' Sense of Belonging

Community is the foundation. Belonging is what grows from it. And community does not build itself.

How community is built

Most people think community gets built through programs. Events. Activities. Gatherings. And yes, those things matter. But they are only half the picture.

Community is built through two types of connection.Individual connection, and group connection.
Type 01

Individual connections

One-on-one. The knock on the door. The hallway conversation. The moment a staff member notices a resident who has not been around lately and checks in. Individual connections are relational and direct: one person reaching out to another. They do not need an audience. They do not need an occasion. They just need intention. A deliberate choice to make someone feel seen.

Type 02

Group connections

Shared. The floor dinner, the community program, the event where a group of residents experience something together. Group connections create a sense of shared identity, the feeling that we are part of the same community.

Both types of connection matter. Neither one is optional.But they do not work the same way, and they do not reach the same residents.

Not every resident starts in the same place

Key concept

Sphere of connection

Our way of describing belonging as a multidimensional space. Residents arrive at college from infinitely different starting points, shaped by identity, background, and prior experience, and meet community in different ways from those different starting points. We are not building a single line that everyone walks. We are meeting people across a sphere.

Here is the insight most community-building models miss.

Students do not all arrive at the same point on the sphere of connection.

Belonging does not live on a line. It lives on a sphere. A sphere has no fixed edges. No single direction. No beginning or end. A sphere holds infinite points, and every resident arrives at a different one. No two residents land in exactly the same place.

Some residents move in ready for community. They are socially confident. They introduce themselves quickly. They are comfortable walking into a room full of strangers. For those residents, a floor game night or a community event is the perfect entry point.

Other residents arrive differently. They are quiet. Uncertain. Maybe lonely. And they would never walk into a big event as their first introduction to community. For them, a group connection is not a bridge to belonging. It might actually feel like a wall.

Now here is something important. Where a resident lands on the sphere is not random. Research, including Terrell Strayhorn's work on belonging for underrepresented students, tells us that race, ethnicity, first-generation status, international student experience, disability, gender identity, and sexual orientation all shape how residents arrive at community. Students from historically marginalized backgrounds may carry prior experiences of exclusion with them. Those experiences can make it slower for them to trust institutional environments. Recognizing this is not about labeling residents. It is about understanding that the sphere has structural dimensions, not just personality ones.

Think about the resident who has not left their room in a week. That resident does not need a flyer on their door. They need someone to notice them. They need an individual connection. A direct, human, one-on-one moment where someone reached out for them specifically.

A program reaches the residents who already feel safe enough to show up.An individual connection reaches the resident who is still deciding whether this community is for them.

Belonging includes you

Building community is real labor: emotional, relational, and mostly invisible. Noticing, holding, listening, returning, day after day. Most of what makes a community feel like a community never shows up in a report.

That kind of work costs something. The pillar that builds belonging in residents has to take seriously the wellbeing of the people building it.

Sustainable presence means three things

1

Know what you can carry

A community needs staff who are reliably present, not heroically present and then gone. Pick a level of engagement you can hold across the whole year, not just the first two weeks.

2

Recognize when you are depleted

You will be. When conversations that used to feel easy start to drain you, that is information. Pull back, ask for support, take the rest you need.

3

Trust that you are not the whole network

You are one node. A resident you cannot reach today might be reached by someone else this week. That is the system working as designed.

A community sustained by staff who burn out is not a sustainable community.The work asks something real of you. It also asks you to take care of the person doing it.
Closing synthesis

What community actually requires

Belonging is what community makes possible. And community itself is built through two types of connection working together. Group connections create shared identity and belonging at scale. Individual connections build trust. They reach isolated residents. They establish the relational foundation that makes group connection meaningful.

But belonging is not a place every resident arrives at from the same starting point. It lives on a sphere with infinite points. Where a resident lands is shaped by who they are, where they have been, and what they carry with them.

And the staff member building all of this is part of what they are building. The pillar holds belonging for residents. It also has to hold belonging for the people doing the work.

Three things about residents. What community makes possible. How community is built. Where residents arrive. And one thing about you. You belong here too. That is where ResLife Connects begins.

C 103

Inclusive engagement design framework

A three-part framework (access, belonging, and invitation) for designing programs that every resident can actually show up to and belong in.

Learning objectives
  1. Explain what inclusive engagement design is and why it is central to building real community.
  2. Identify the three dimensions of inclusive design (access, belonging, and invitation) and the questions that guide each one.
  3. Recognize when and how to use the inclusive design questions across the program lifecycle.

What inclusive engagement design is

Every program sends a signal. Before a resident even walks in, it tells them whether this program was designed for them, or for somebody else.

Inclusive engagement design is the practice of making sure that signal lands for every resident.Not just the ones who already feel safe showing up.

Residents arrive at infinitely different points on the sphere of connection, shaped by who they are and what they carry with them. Inclusive design is the operational answer to that. It is how we build programs that can actually meet residents where they are, instead of where it would be convenient for them to be.

There are three parts to this work. Think of them as a journey every resident takes on their way to actually showing up.

1

Access

Can a resident physically, sensorially, and technically participate?

2

Belonging

Does the program fit who they actually are and how they actually engage?

3

Invitation

Have you personally reached out to the residents who most need to be there?

Each part has questions inside it. Let's walk through them.

Access

The question: Can every resident get to the program and engage with it?

Access is the ground floor. Before a program can include anyone, residents have to be able to reach it, sense it, and operate the technology that connects them to it.

Location and physical accessibility

Can every resident physically get to this space and move through it comfortably?

Start with the space itself. Can a resident using a wheelchair, crutches, or any mobility aid actually reach the room? Are there ramps, elevators, and accessible seating? Is there an accessible restroom nearby?

Then look at the path. Is it well-lit, safely walkable after dark, and clearly signed? Is the location itself remote enough to exclude residents without transportation?

And think about flow. Once someone is in the space, can they move comfortably? Can they sit when they need to sit, stand when they need to stand, and leave without making a scene if they need to go?

Physical accessibility is not just about who can enter. It is about who can stay comfortably once they are there.

Sensory and cognitive accessibility

Can residents with different sensory and cognitive needs engage without barriers?

Start with the environment. How loud is the space? How bright are the lights? Is there a lower-stimulation corner built in, or a break area where residents can step away if they need to reset?

Then look at the content. Are all videos captioned? Are visuals explained verbally? Are instructions concrete and clear, and do you repeat them in more than one format?

And is the structure predictable? Residents who rely on predictability to engage need to know how the program starts, how it ends, and what is happening in between.

Digital and technological inclusion

Can residents participate regardless of their tech access or digital comfort?

Not every resident has a laptop. Not every resident has a smartphone with reliable data. Not every resident is comfortable navigating digital platforms.

Start by asking what the program actually requires. Does it need a specific app? A stable internet connection? A device a resident might not own? If so, can you offer a non-digital alternative?

Look at sign-up and registration. Is the process accessible, or does it gatekeep residents who do not have the right device or account?

And do not assume one platform reaches everyone. Some residents use email. Some use GroupMe. Some use Instagram. Some check the flyers in the hallway. Promote across platforms, not just the one you use most.

Access is the first question, because nothing else matters if residents cannot get in.

Belonging

The question: Does the program fit who residents actually are and how they engage?

Access gets residents in the room. Belonging makes sure the room was actually built for them.

Cultural and religious inclusion

Does this program respect and accommodate the cultural and religious realities of this community?

Our residents come from everywhere. Different cultures. Different religions. Different traditions. Different expectations of what a community program even looks like.

Start with timing. Are you avoiding planning with major religious holidays in mind?

Then look at food. If there is food, does it accommodate halal, kosher, vegetarian, vegan, and allergen-free needs by default, not as a special request?

And look at the content itself. Do your examples, stories, and references go beyond a single cultural frame? Do they assume American, Western, or mainstream norms that some residents will not share?

Socioeconomic inclusion

Can residents participate fully regardless of financial means?

Not every resident has disposable income. Not every resident has a car. Not every resident has parents covering expenses.

Start with cost. Is the program free? If there is any cost at all, is it clearly optional, and is opting out invisible?

Then think about what participation actually requires. Supplies. Technology. Transportation. Clothing. If any of those are needed, make sure they are provided, not assumed.

And check your content. Does it assume baselines like going home for break, family vacations, or hobbies that cost money?

Format and participation modes

Are there multiple ways to engage that match different comfort levels?

A program built on one mode of participation only reaches residents whose comfort matches that mode.

Can a resident engage by watching? By writing? By talking? By doing something with their hands? By contributing virtually? By staying quiet in the corner and still feeling like they are part of the program?

Think about entry points. Can a resident show up for ten minutes and leave without making it awkward? Can they join late without drawing attention? Can they participate anonymously or semi-anonymously if they want to?

And think about roles. Some residents want to be attendees. Some want to volunteer. Some want to help run it. The more varied the roles, the more residents can find a way in.

Neurodivergent-inclusive design

Does the structure support residents who engage differently from the default?

Neurodivergent residents (including residents with ADHD, autism, sensory processing differences, and other ways of engaging with the world) often thrive in community when the structure makes room for how they actually work.

Start by making expectations explicit. Share the agenda, the structure, the length, and the vibe of the program in advance.

Build in room for unstructured engagement. Quiet corners. Solo tasks. Parallel participation, where a resident can be present without being required to socialize constantly.

Signal transitions clearly. When you are shifting from one part of the program to another, say so. Do not just move the group without warning.

And remember that socializing is not the only form of engagement. A resident who is present, quiet, and engaged with the activity is participating. Let them.

Belonging is about designing a program that does not make any resident feel like they are visiting someone else's community.It can be challenging to incorporate every element consistently, but our expectation is that we stay intentional and do our best each time.

Invitation

The question: Have you personally invited the residents who most need to hear the invitation?

This is the last part, and it might be the most important.

You can build the most inclusive program in the world. You can design around every accessibility need, every cultural consideration, every format preference. And you can still end up with the same fifteen residents who always show up.

Well-designed programs reach the residents who already feel safe enough to show up.They do not automatically reach residents who do not. Individual invitations do.

Before the program, ask yourself who you most want to be there. Name them. The quiet resident on the second floor. The resident you have not seen in a while. The resident from an underrepresented group who has not been to anything yet. The resident you have been meaning to check in on.

Then go invite them. In person if you can. A knock on the door. A conversation in the hallway. A direct message that says, "I'd really like for you to come to this."

Not a flyer. Not a mass email. A personal invitation.

This is where inclusive design meets individual connection. The design gets residents in the door. The invitation gives them a reason to walk through it.

Invitation is what turns a well-designed program into a full one.
Closing synthesis

Access, belonging, invitation

Inclusive design is not a checklist you run after the fact to make sure you did not miss anyone. It is how you plan from the start.

Three questions. Asked before you design, while you design, and after you execute.

Access. Can every resident physically, sensorially, and technically participate?

Belonging. Does the program fit who they are and how they engage?

Invitation. Have you personally invited the residents who most need to hear it?

When the answer to all three is yes, you have built something that has a real chance of reaching the whole community. Not just the part of it that already shows up.

That is what inclusive design actually is. And it is what Res Life Connects requires.

Step 01

Spot the patterns

Here is the counterintuitive part. UCLA researchers built the loneliness scale that is still used worldwide, and the finding it kept turning up was not that big programs cured loneliness. It was that students felt less lonely when someone reliably noticed them.

Individual connections are not the soft side of the work.They are the work.

Before you can reach the people you serve, you have to notice them. Individual connection starts with attention. Who is showing up? Who has disappeared? Who is quiet in a way they were not before?

The staff who build strong individual connections are not the ones who run the most programs. They are the ones who notice.

Spotting patterns means paying attention to the spaces your unit operates. It means keeping a mental roster of who you have seen and when. It means noticing when someone who used to be around is not anymore.

Important This is not surveillance. It is care. You are not tracking people. You are holding space for them in your attention so that when something shifts, you catch it.

Look for three patterns: who is consistently present, who is consistently absent, and who is changing. Any of those three is a signal worth following up on.

Step 02

Extend the invitation

The people you serve rarely come to us. They do not walk up to the desk. They do not knock on the door. They do not raise their hand.

That is not because they do not want connection. It is because asking for connection feels vulnerable, and most are not sure if we will be warm when they do. So they wait.

Individual connection requires that we go first.

An invitation does not have to be formal. It can be a hallway hello that names them by name. A knock on the door. A quick text that says, "I haven't seen you in a bit, how are you?" It can be sitting down with someone in a shared space.

The point is the move. You made it first. They did not have to.

Most of the time, residents you invite will say yes. Sometimes they will say no. Both are fine. What matters is that you asked.

Step 03

Engage the person

Once someone says yes, the work is the conversation itself. Engagement is the craft of being present with one person. It is asking open questions. It is listening more than you talk. It is being curious about who they actually are, not who you assumed they were.

Open questions

Open questions do not have a yes or no answer. "How's it going?" is closed in practice, because the answer is almost always "fine." Try "What's been on your mind this week?" or "What's something you've been enjoying lately?" Questions that give the resident somewhere to go.

Listen for what they care about

The show they are watching. The class they are stressed about. The friend they mentioned. These are not small details. They are what you will reference later to show you were paying attention.

Learn something

Every conversation should leave you knowing something about them you did not know before. If it did not, you were talking too much.

Step 04

Nurture the relationship

One conversation is not a relationship. It is a start. The fourth step is the one most staff skip. You had a good conversation. You learned something. Then you never followed up, and the next time you see them it feels like starting over.

Come back

Reference what you learned. "How did that exam go?" "Did you end up finishing that series?" "How's your roommate situation these days?"

Remember

Write things down if you have to. A quick note in your phone after a conversation. A name, a detail, a thing they said that mattered to them.

Show up repeatedly

Individual connection compounds. The fifth conversation is richer than the first. The tenth is richer than the fifth.

Residents trust staff they see over and over again.Not staff they see once.
Closing synthesis

The compounding work

Four steps. Spot. Invite. Engage. Nurture.

Each one alone is small. A glance down the hallway. A hello. A question. A follow-up. None of them look like much.

But done over weeks and months, with one resident after another, they are how community actually gets built. Not through big programs. Through a thousand small acts of attention.

Group connection reaches residents at scale. Individual connection is what gives those programs their pull. When a resident shows up to your event, it is usually because of a relationship, not a flyer.

Start with noticing. Make the first move. Be present when they are with you. Come back. That is it.

C 303

Identify the need

How to read your community, distinguish loud needs from quiet ones, and turn observations into a clear programming question. The first step of every group program.

Learning objectives
  1. Read your community for the signals that surface a programming need.
  2. Distinguish loud needs (situational and obvious) from quiet needs (slow and structural), and program to both.
  3. Turn observation into a clear programming question that the rest of the design process can answer.

Reading your community

Every program should start with a question: what does this community actually need right now?

Not what you want to do. Not what is on the department calendar. Not what ran last year. What this community, this week, actually needs.

Identifying the need means reading your community on purpose. It looks like:

  • Listening in lounges and hallways. Noticing what residents are talking about and what they are not.
  • Checking in with staff colleagues about what they are hearing in the spaces you do not see.
  • Watching attendance patterns. Who shows up to programs? Who does not? Who used to and stopped?
  • Tracking your own gut. If something feels off in the community, that is information.
The staff who run programs that land are the staff who are paying attention before they design.

Loud needs and quiet needs

Needs in residential communities come in two shapes.

Type 01

Loud needs

The ones you can name out loud. Midterms are coming and residents are stressed. A conflict in the building has people on edge. A holiday is approaching and residents who will not be going home are feeling it. Move-in week, finals week, the weekend a major event hits the news cycle.

Loud needs are easy to spot. The harder part is moving fast enough to program to them while they are still active.

Type 02

Quiet needs

The ones underneath the surface. People seem disconnected from each other. The community feels flat. No one is hanging out in common spaces. Roommates pass each other in the hallway without speaking. New residents have stopped trying to plug in.

Quiet needs are the ones that make a community feel like a community when they are addressed, and feel like a building when they are not. Most quiet needs are slow to surface and slow to resolve. They are rarely solved by one program. They require a programming arc.

Both kinds of needs are worth programming to.The first step is seeing them.

From observation to a programming question

Observation is not the same as a need. Observation becomes a need when you can put it in a sentence.

The sentence shape

"Right now, [these residents] are experiencing [this thing]. A program could help by [doing this]."

If you can fill in those three blanks, you have something the rest of the design process can answer. If you cannot, you are not ready to design yet, you are ready to keep observing.

Some examples:

"Right now, first-years on the second floor haven't met each other yet. A program could help by giving them a structured way to introduce themselves."
"Right now, residents are stressed about finals. A program could help by offering a low-stakes break from studying that doesn't add to their load."
"Right now, the community feels disconnected. A program could help by surfacing one shared experience residents can talk about afterward."
The sharper the sentence, the easier every step that comes after gets.
Closing synthesis

See before you plan

Programs that miss usually miss at this step. The design was thoughtful. The flyer looked good. The space was reserved. But the program answered a question no one in the community was asking.

Programs that land start here. Read your community. Notice the loud needs and the quiet ones. Turn what you are seeing into a sentence you can build a program around.

You cannot design a program that meets a need until you know what the need is.

C 304

Design the experience

How to design a program from end to end: securing the approvals it needs, planning the experience itself, and promoting it so residents actually show up.

Learning objectives
  1. Navigate the approvals every program must move through and build a realistic timeline that accounts for them.
  2. Plan a program that starts with the audience and outcome, picks a modality that fits, and flows in a way that gives every resident a way in.
  3. Promote across the platforms residents actually use, give your invitation lead time, and let personal invitation do the heavy lifting.

Securing approvals

Before a program can run, it has to be approved.

That can feel like bureaucracy, and sometimes it is. But most of the time, the approval process exists for real reasons. Budgets need to balance. Spaces need to be reserved so two programs do not collide. Risks need to be assessed so residents are safe. Co-sponsors need to know what they are signing up for.

Approvals are not the enemy of programming.They are the operating system of programming.

The approvals your program will likely need

Supervisor approval

Your direct supervisor or program coordinator needs to know what you are planning, when, and why. This is the first conversation, and it is where most programs get shaped before they are submitted anywhere else.

Budget approval

If your program costs money, you will need to request funding through your unit's budget process. That means knowing what it costs, what the money is for, and who is authorizing it.

Space reservation

Whether you are using a lounge, a multipurpose room, or an outdoor space, reserve it through whatever system your unit uses. Do not assume a space is available because it is usually empty.

Co-sponsorship coordination

If you are partnering with another unit or campus department, they will need time to sign off on their piece, usually more than you expect.

Risk and liability review

Anything involving food, physical activity, transportation, or off-campus travel will need to move through a risk review. Start this early.

Lead time is the variable that breaks programs.Most programs need a minimum of three weeks of lead time from the first approval conversation to the day of the program. Programs with food, transportation, or off-campus travel need more.

Start with your program date and work backwards. Submit early. Follow up. An approval sitting in someone's inbox is still an unapproved program.

The reframe Approvals done well are not overhead. They are how you become someone the department can count on.

Planning the experience

Once your approvals are moving, plan the program itself. Most programs that fall flat fell flat at the planning stage, long before the day of the event. The most common mistake is starting with the activity. Someone says "let's do a paint night" and the whole plan flows from that. What is missing are the two questions that should come first.

Who is it for, and what does it create?

Not "residents." That is too broad. Which residents? First-years who have not made friends yet? Transfer students looking for community? Residents who are stressed about midterms? The answer changes everything about the program.

And what does it create? Not what the program is. What the experience produces. Is this a program that helps residents meet each other? A program that gives them a break from academic pressure? A program that celebrates a community milestone?

When you know who it is for and what it creates, the activity becomes easy to pick.When you do not, any activity will feel like a guess.

Choose a modality that fits

Every program is a modality choice, whether you make it consciously or not. Two dimensions to consider:

Dimension 01

Active or passive

Active programming is facilitated in the moment: staff present, human energy required (study breaks, game nights, discussions). Passive programming runs on its own: no facilitation needed, residents engage on their own terms (bulletin boards, lounge setups, GroupMe chats).

Dimension 02

Physical or virtual

Physical programming happens in shared physical space. Virtual happens in digital spaces residents already use: group chats, social media, shared playlists, email threads.

Most staff default to active-physical without thinking, because that is what "programming" looks like in their head. But a lot of the best community work is passive, virtual, or both. A well-maintained GroupMe with real community energy might reach more residents than a Tuesday night study break.

Design the flow

Once you know the audience, the outcome, and the modality, design how the program moves from start to finish. The first five minutes as residents arrive. The transition from small talk into the main activity. The moment you decide to wind down. The goodbye.

Design the first five minutes especially carefully. Those minutes determine whether residents stay. Will they walk into a room where everyone already knows each other, or one that has clear space for them? Will they know what to do, or stand awkwardly near the food table? Will they feel welcome by name, or like they crashed someone else's event?

Give residents a way in at every stage. Some want to be there an hour early helping set up. Some want to slide in halfway through and leave when they want to. A good program has entry points throughout, not just at the start.

And design an exit that is not awkward. Residents should be able to leave without feeling like they are abandoning you. The ones who want to stay will stay. The ones who need to go will feel good about having come.

Promoting the program

A planned program that no one knows about is not really a program.

Promote where residents actually are

The first mistake most staff make is promoting where they hang out, not where residents do. Residents are not all on the same platform. Some check email the moment it arrives. Others have not opened a ResLife email since move-in. Some live in GroupMe. Some are only reachable through Instagram. Some only read the flyers in the elevator.

Promotion has to cross platforms. Email for the email-readers. Group chat for the group-chat-livers. Flyers for the residents who walk past them. Word of mouth for the residents who trust their friends more than they trust us.

Equity Residents with different access to technology, different comfort with platforms, and different cultural and language needs are reached through different channels. Promote like you believe that.

Personal invitation outperforms any flyer

A flyer is an announcement. An invitation is a relationship.

Most people who walk past your flyer will not come to your program. Most people you personally invite will at least consider it. The difference is who made the effort. The flyer is effort you put into the space. The invitation is effort you put into them.

This is where individual relationships and group programming meet. The people you have built one-on-one connections with are the ones who will show up, because when you invite them personally, the invitation is not from a random staff member running an event. It is from someone they already know.

Before you finalize your promotion plan, make a list. Who are the people you would most like to attend? Name them. Then invite them directly. Knock on a door, send a text, stop them in the hallway, drop by their office.

"I'm running this on Thursday. I'd really like for you to come."

Lead time and cadence

Even the best promotion fails if it arrives too late or too little. Residents do not commit the first time they hear about a program. They need to see it more than once, over multiple days, before it registers. If your promotion starts the day before, most residents will not see it in time.

A good rule: start promotion at least a week out.

Day 7 Post it.
Day 4 Remind people.
Day 1 Personal invitations to the residents you most want there.
Day of A final nudge in the group chat.

After you promote, do not assume anyone has seen it. Ask them. "Did you see the thing I'm doing Thursday?" Most will say no. That is how you know your promotion is not working as hard as you are.

Closing synthesis

Design is a choice

Every program is designed. The only question is whether it is designed on purpose.

The staff who design on purpose secure the approvals early so they are not the constraint. They start with the audience and the outcome, not the activity. They pick a modality that fits. They map the flow before they build the Canva flyer. They promote where residents actually are, with enough lead time to reach them, and they let personal invitation do the heavy lifting.

The staff who do not design on purpose start with the activity, scramble for approvals, and promote the day before. Sometimes it works. Usually it does not.

Design well across all three steps, and the program shows up to its own event. So do the residents.

Execute with presence

How to make the environment you create the program itself. Reading the room, staying present, and recovering when a program does not go as planned.

Learning objectives
  1. Recognize that the environment you create is often more important than the activity itself.
  2. Read the room in real time and adjust what is happening as needed.
  3. Recover gracefully when the program does not go as planned.

The environment is the program

Residents do not remember the activity.They remember how the room felt.

You can run the same program twice with the same materials and the same plan, and one version feels warm while the other feels flat. The difference is not the design. It is the energy you brought to it.

Execution with presence starts before the first resident arrives. Is the room set up with intention? Is the music right for the vibe? Are you ready, or are you still scrambling? When a resident walks in, what do they pick up from your body, your face, your voice?

Staff who execute well are glad to be there, and residents feel it. Staff who are rushed, distracted, or checked out are giving the program away even if the activity is perfect on paper.

You are the program.The activity is just the container.

Reading the room

Once residents arrive, the program stops being what you planned and starts being what is actually happening.

Reading the room means noticing. Are people engaging, or standing around awkwardly? Is the conversation flowing, or forced? Are residents leaving, or staying past the end?

Each of those signals is telling you something.

  • If it is awkward, the activity is not working.
  • If people are leaving, the program has gone on too long.
  • If conversation is flowing, get out of the way and let it keep flowing.

Reading the room also means noticing individuals. The resident in the corner who has not talked to anyone. The resident who is clearly about to leave. The resident who seems excited but does not know where to plug in. Those are moments that need you specifically.

Be willing to adjust.If the plan is not working, change the plan. If a conversation is better than the activity, skip the activity. The program is what is in the room right now, not what is on the sheet.

Recovery

Something always goes wrong.

The food did not show up. The activity did not go well. Only two residents came. The speaker did not prepare. The projector broke.

How you recover matters more than what went wrong.

The staff who recover well share a few habits:

  • They do not panic, or at least do not show it.
  • They acknowledge the problem without dwelling on it.
  • They pivot to something that can work with what they have.
  • They laugh when something is genuinely funny about the situation.
Residents are not looking for a perfect program.They are looking for someone who can handle it when things get weird. That is worth more than any flawless event.

The programs your residents remember will not always be the smooth ones. Sometimes they will be the ones that almost fell apart and didn't, because you were there.

Closing synthesis

Presence over polish

You do not need to run polished programs. You need to run present ones.

Presence means being there, fully. Being ready. Paying attention. Adjusting when needed. Recovering when things slip. Bringing energy that residents can feel.

A program executed with presence and a small hiccup beats a program executed mechanically and on plan. Every time.

The environment you create is the program. Create one residents want to be in.

  2. Gather resident feedback without over-surveying your community.

Reflecting before you forget

Every program teaches you something.But only if you write it down.

The first hour after a program is when you remember the most. What worked. What did not. Who showed up that you were not expecting. Who did not show up that you were. The moment someone said something that stuck with you. The part where you lost the room for fifteen minutes.

Most of that disappears within a day.

Build a reflection habit. Something simple. A five-minute note in your phone after every program. A quick check-in with your co-facilitator on the walk back. Three bullets written down before you sleep.

The questions do not have to be complicated.

Three questions
What worked?
What didn't?
What would I change next time?

If you write just those three things, you are ahead of most staff.

Reflect honestly.Not every program is going to be a hit. Some are going to flop. Writing that down is not failure. It is the data you need to do it differently next time.

Asking residents what they think

Residents have information you do not. But they will only give it to you if you ask well.

Ask less, and ask better questions.

Pick the question that fits the program and ask it once.

"What's one thing you liked?"
"What's one thing you'd change?"
"Would you come back?"

Ask in the moment when you can. As residents are leaving, ask one person directly. You will get more honest answers in a doorway conversation than in any form.

Do not ask if you are not going to do something with the answer.Feedback you do not act on is worse than no feedback, because residents learn their input does not matter.

Feeding it forward

The last piece of assessment is the one most staff skip.

You reflected. You got feedback. Now what?

Now it has to change something. The next program has to be different because of what you learned from this one. If it isn't, the whole assessment process was performance.

Feeding it forward means carrying your reflection into the design of the next program:

  • The activity that did not work gets replaced.
  • The promotion channel that did not reach anyone gets abandoned.
  • The timing that was wrong gets moved.
  • The thing a resident said they wanted gets built into the next one.

It also means sharing forward. Your colleagues are running programs too. The lessons you are learning could save them from learning the same ones the hard way. Pass it on in staff meetings, team texts, whatever forum your unit uses.

Programs improve faster across a team than they do alone.
Closing synthesis

The loop

Four steps. Identify. Design. Execute. Assess.

And then back to identify, because what you just learned from this program is telling you something about what the community needs next.

Assessment closes the loop. It is the moment where a single program stops being a single program and starts being part of an ongoing practice of building community.

Run a program. Learn from it. Run a better one next time. That is the work.

A wellness-first lens

UCLA's framing of student well-being is intentionally broad. Through Be Well Bruin (UCLA Student Affairs' central wellness directory launched in Fall 2023), the university recognizes that student well-being is a holistic experience, not just mental health.

Be Well Bruin organizes wellness across five dimensions:

Physical

Quality of life through harm prevention, active living, nutrition, sleep, substance use, and sexual health.

Academic

Managing workload while balancing satisfaction in program, performance, and progress.

Emotional

The way mental, psychological, and social well-being shapes thoughts, feelings, and actions.

Financial

Financial knowledge and access to support that helps students reach their goals.

Social

Building healthy, nurturing, and supportive relationships and genuine connection.

A student in crisis is rarely struggling in just one dimension. A financial shock often becomes an emotional one. Academic pressure leaks into sleep, nutrition, and isolation. The wellness lens helps you ask better questions and make better referrals, because the right entry point depends on the dimension under stress.

This module trains you to assist Bruins in crisis.Be Well Bruin is the directory you point them toward. CAPS, CORE, RISE, the Dean of Students, and Case Management are the partners you escalate to.

The spectrum of student distress

Distress is not a single state. It exists on a spectrum, and where a student sits on that spectrum determines how you respond.

Mild distress

Stress, fatigue, homesickness, frustration, loneliness. The student is functioning but struggling.

Many students at this level benefit from RISE Center workshops, HEART programming, peer support, and self-directed Be Well Bruin tools.

Moderate distress

Sustained sadness, withdrawal, declining academic performance, escalating conflict, sleep and appetite changes, loss of motivation. The student is impaired but not unsafe.

CAPS therapy, RISE Health & Wellness Coaching, Case Management, and dimension-specific resources (financial aid, academic advising) are appropriate.

Severe distress / crisis

Hopelessness, suicidal ideation, psychosis, aggression, inability to care for self, immediate safety concern.

Move directly to the CAPS 24/7 Crisis Line (310-825-0768), CORE, 988, and/or UCPD.
Your job Not to diagnose. Recognize where on the spectrum a student appears to be, and match your response and referral to that level.

Early warning signs across the five dimensions

You see things no one else sees: hallway conversations, programming attendance, roommate dynamics, midnight kitchens. You are positioned to notice early. The five wellness dimensions sharpen what you are looking for.

Emotional

Emotional signs

  • Flat affect or unusual emotional volatility
  • Statements of hopelessness, worthlessness, or being a burden
  • Talk of "going home for good," disappearing, or not being missed
Academic

Academic signs

  • Disappearing from class
  • Sudden drop in performance
  • Talking about giving up on a major or college altogether
Physical

Physical signs

  • Visible weight change
  • Decline in self-care
  • Sleeping all day or not at all
  • Unexplained injuries
  • Increased substance use
Social

Social signs

  • Withdrawing from friends, floor, and community
  • Conflict spikes with roommates or partners
  • Isolation in the room for extended periods
  • Giving away meaningful possessions
Financial

Financial signs

  • Skipping meals or scarcity around food
  • Talk of dropping out for financial reasons
  • Job loss, family financial shock, or housing insecurity
One sign is information. A pattern across dimensions is a signal.A pattern plus a direct statement is a call to act.

The intervention conversation

When you decide to engage, do so with intention. The structure is simple: Notice. Name. Connect.

Notice

01

Approach privately. Choose a moment when the student has time and space. Lead with what you have observed, not what you have concluded.

"I noticed you've been keeping your door closed more than usual, and I haven't seen you at any community events the past two weeks. I wanted to check in."

Name

02

Make space for the student to share. Listen more than you talk. Validate the emotional reality without minimizing or fixing.

"That sounds really heavy. Thank you for telling me."

Connect

03

Move toward support. You are not the resource, you are the bridge. Offer specific options drawn from Be Well Bruin.

"There's a CAPS 24/7 crisis line at 310-825-0768. CORE can come to you on campus for a wellness check. The RISE Center has same-week drop-in sessions. I can sit with you while you call, or walk you over. What feels manageable right now?"
Boundaries to hold

What not to do

  • Do not promise confidentiality.
  • Do not promise outcomes.
  • Do not investigate or diagnose.
  • Do not leave the conversation without a next step.

Mental health & suicide prevention

Deliver Suicide Safe and ABC training and practice response to mental health emergencies. Build confidence for the highest-stakes moments.

A note on this material

This module covers suicide and mental health in detail. If anything in this content connects to your own experience and you would like support, please reach out to CAPS, the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, or a trusted person in your life.

Learning objectives
  1. Recognize warning signs and risk factors for suicide and severe mental health crises.
  2. Apply the Suicide Safe and ABC (Assess, Build, Connect) protocols when responding.
  3. Connect students in mental health emergencies to CAPS, CORE, UCPD, and emergency services.

The scope of student mental health

College student mental health is at historic levels of need. National surveys have consistently shown that:

  • A majority of college students report moderate to serious psychological distress in any given year.
  • Anxiety and depression remain the two most-reported concerns.
  • A meaningful percentage of students report suicidal ideation each year, and a smaller but significant percentage report a suicide attempt during their college experience.

For UCLA Residential Life, this is not a statistic. It is the lived reality of our communities.

You are not a clinician. You are also not a bystander.You are a trained first responder for the first sixty seconds of a mental health moment, and the bridge to the people who can carry it from there.

Recognizing risk

Warning signs and risk factors operate at different levels.

Warning signs Acute, situational
  • Talking or writing about wanting to die or kill oneself
  • Looking for ways to end one's life
  • Talking about feeling hopeless, trapped, or being a burden
  • Increasing alcohol or drug use
  • Withdrawing from friends, family, and activities
  • Acting anxious, agitated, or reckless
  • Sleeping too little or too much
  • Mood changes, especially relief after a period of depression
  • Giving away possessions or saying goodbye
Risk factors Background, elevated baseline
  • Prior suicide attempt
  • Mental health condition, especially with limited treatment access
  • Recent loss: relationship, family, identity, status
  • Family history of suicide
  • Access to lethal means
  • Chronic pain or serious illness
  • Identity-based marginalization or recent trauma
Warning signs are urgent. Risk factors elevate baseline concern.Either can prompt action.

The Suicide Safe protocol

When you suspect a student may be at risk, ask directly. Asking does not plant the idea. Avoiding the question does not protect the student, it isolates them.

1

Ask directly

"Are you thinking about suicide?"

Use the word. Do not euphemize.

2

Listen without judgment

Let the student describe what they are experiencing. Resist the urge to fix or minimize.

3

Keep them safe

Stay with them. Do not leave them alone if they have indicated suicidal intent.

4

Be there

Acknowledge what they are carrying. Validate the courage it takes to share.

5

Help them connect

Move directly toward CAPS, CORE, UCPD, or 911, based on level of acuity. Sit with them through the call.

6

Follow up and escalate

Notify your supervisor or on-call leadership immediately. Document objectively. Plan a follow-up check-in.

The ABC method

For mental health situations that are concerning but not immediately life-threatening, use ABC.

A

Assess

  • What is the student saying?
  • What is their affect, posture, energy?
  • What do you know about their history and recent events?
  • Is there imminent risk?
B

Build

  • Build connection before solutions.
  • Sit at their level. Reduce distractions.
  • Use their name. Be still.
  • Validate the emotional reality.
C

Connect

  • Connect them to the next layer of support.
  • Offer specific, concrete options, not just "you should talk to someone."
  • Stay with them through the handoff.
  • Document and escalate.
ABC works for the moderate-distress conversations that happen far more often than acute crisis.Suicide Safe works for the moments that demand emergency response. Both are part of the duty toolkit.

After a mental health response

The response is not over when the student is safe in the moment.

Documentation

Objective, third-person, factual. What was observed. What was said. What action was taken. No clinical interpretation.

Escalation

Notify your supervisor or on-call leadership. Senior leadership engages based on protocol. Cases involving sustained risk are routed to Case Management and Dean of Students.

Follow-up

A short check-in 24 to 48 hours later is meaningful. Do not vanish after the crisis passes.

Self-care for staff

These responses leave a residue. Use the debrief structure. Talk with your supervisor. Use staff support resources. Notice your own signs of distress.

You cannot show up for the next resident if you are running on empty.
Closing synthesis

The highest-stakes moments

Suicide and severe mental health crises are the highest-stakes moments in our work. They demand both training and humanity, and you bring both into the room.

Recognize. Ask. Stay. Connect. Document. Care for yourself.

Done with intention, this work saves lives. That is not a metaphor. It is the job.

HEART & bystander intervention

  2. Apply naloxone administration and fentanyl test strip protocols when responding to opioid-related incidents.
  3. Apply the Five Ds of bystander intervention (Direct, Distract, Delegate, Delay, Document) to step in safely during unsafe situations.

Meet HEART, your harm reduction partner

Health Education and Resource Team (HEART)

A department of the Arthur Ashe Student Health and Wellness Center

Formerly known as SHEP, HEART operates with a team of ten Peer Health Educators and a collective-impact approach to health education and harm reduction.

HEART's four focus areas
  • Sexual health
  • Body image and nutrition
  • Alcohol and other drugs
  • Health literacy
What HEART offers ResLife
  • Required naloxone (Narcan) training as part of ResLife onboarding.
  • Fentanyl test strips and education at 20+ campus distribution points through the UCLA Narcan Distribution Project.
  • Workshops and tabling on request, for floors, buildings, halls, or staff teams.
  • Peer-led programming you can co-design.
  • Ongoing partnership for harm-reduction strategy in your community.
Why naloxone training is now standard CDC data has documented a sustained increase in fentanyl-driven opioid deaths since 2018, with synthetic opioids now responsible for the majority of overdose fatalities in the United States. California responded with AB 461, the Campus Opioid Safety Act (2022), requiring public colleges and universities to make naloxone available and train residence-life staff on its use. Your training is the operational implementation of that requirement.

Opioid overdose & naloxone (Narcan)

Opioid overdose is reversible if naloxone is administered quickly.

What naloxone does

Naloxone (brand name Narcan) is an opiate overdose reversal medication delivered as a nasal spray. It rapidly displaces opioids from receptor sites, restoring breathing in someone in active overdose. It works on heroin, fentanyl, prescription opioids, and most synthetic opioids.

Recognizing an overdose
  • Slow, shallow, or stopped breathing
  • Blue or grayish lips, fingertips, skin
  • Unresponsive to voice or touch
  • Choking or gurgling sounds
  • Pinpoint pupils
  • Limp body

The response sequence

1

Call 911

Immediately. California's 911 Good Samaritan Law protects callers from low-level drug charges in overdose situations.

2

Administer naloxone

Following your training. Tilt the head back, insert the nasal applicator, press the plunger.

3

Begin rescue breathing

If the person is not breathing.

4

Stay with the person

Naloxone wears off in 30 to 90 minutes. If opioids are still in their system, overdose can return. A second dose may be needed.

5

Place them in the recovery position

On their side, once they resume breathing.

6

Notify and document

Notify your supervisor or on-call leadership and document objectively.

Know the closest dose Naloxone is available at 20+ campus locations through the UCLA Narcan Distribution Project. Know where the closest dose to your community lives before you need it.

Fentanyl awareness & test strips

Fentanyl is a powerful synthetic opioid: 50 times more potent than heroin and 100 times more potent than morphine. It is the leading driver of overdose deaths nationally. Most fentanyl-related deaths are caused by people unknowingly ingesting fentanyl that has been cut into another substance.

What this means for residents

  • Any drug not obtained from a doctor and pharmacy may contain fentanyl.
  • This includes counterfeit prescription pills (fake Xanax, Adderall, Percocet, oxycodone), cocaine, MDMA, and other recreational substances.
  • Visual inspection cannot detect fentanyl. Test strips can.

Fentanyl test strips

HEART distributes fentanyl test strips at the same 20+ campus locations as naloxone. You are required to watch the three-minute test strip video as part of your training to learn the protocol.

The harm reduction message you can deliver

"If you are going to use, test the substance first. Do not use alone. Have naloxone nearby. Know the signs of overdose. Call 911 if something seems off, California's Good Samaritan Law protects you."

This message is not an endorsement of substance use.It is the message that keeps people alive long enough to make different choices later.

The harm reduction philosophy

Harm reduction is the foundation underneath HEART's work, and underneath your role when responding.

Harm reduction means
  • Meeting people where they are, not where you wish they were.
  • Reducing the negative consequences of risky behavior without requiring abstinence.
  • Treating people who use drugs, alcohol, or other substances with dignity.
  • Prioritizing life and safety over judgment.
In residential life, it looks like
  • Carrying naloxone whether or not you support substance use.
  • Distributing test strip information without lecturing.
  • Responding to an overdose with urgency, not moralizing.
  • Documenting medical responses (FYI / Support) and policy violations (Conduct) accurately, and remembering they often coexist.
  • Building relationships where residents feel safe calling for help instead of hiding.
A resident who is afraid of getting written up may not call you when their friend stops breathing.Your job is to be the staff member residents call.

Bystander intervention: the Five Ds

Most harm in residential communities happens within view of someone who could have stepped in. Bystander intervention turns passive witnesses into active protectors. The Five Ds are the most widely used, most evidence-supported framework for stepping in safely.

D

Direct

Address the situation directly when it is safe to do so. Name what you see. State what should change.

"Hey, that joke isn't appropriate."

Use when you have standing, the situation is not violent, and your safety is not at risk.

D

Distract

Interrupt the situation without confrontation. Pull attention. Redirect. Buy time and space.

"Hey, do you have a charger I can borrow?"

Use when a direct approach could escalate, or you want to give the person being targeted an exit.

D

Delegate

Bring in someone with more capacity, authority, or training. The staff on duty, your supervisor, UCPD, security, dispatch, a friend.

"Let me call the staff on duty." (in Residential Education: the Duty RD)

Use when the situation exceeds your scope, requires authority, or carries safety risk.

D

Delay

Check in afterward when direct action was not safe or possible. Do not assume the moment has passed.

"I saw what happened earlier. Are you okay? Do you want to talk about it?"

Use when the moment has passed but the impact has not.

D

Document

Record what you witnessed when it is safe to do so. Time, place, people, what was said and done. Documentation supports formal reports, Title IX cases, conduct processes, and pattern recognition.

Use when documenting will not put you or others at risk in the moment, and afterward when you submit your duty report.

You do not have to use all five.You have to use at least one.
Closing synthesis

HEART + heart

HEART is a partner. Bystander intervention is a practice. Together, they form the harm-reduction half of your role.

Carry naloxone. Know the test strip protocol. Know the Five Ds cold. Build relationships where residents call you instead of hiding from you.

Public health is life-saving, and you are part of that work.

Earthquake & the Great ShakeOut

Drop

Drop onto your hands and knees to protect yourself from being knocked down. Stay low.

Cover

Stay on your knees, bend over to protect your vital organs, and hold on until the shaking stops. If you are under shelter, be ready to move with it. If you are not under shelter, hold on to your head and neck with both arms.

Active threat

Train on active threat protocols, lockdown procedures, and coordinated response with campus police.

Learning objectives
  1. Recognize the difference between a perceived and confirmed active threat.
  2. Apply the Run, Hide, Fight protocol and the residential lockdown procedure.
  3. Coordinate with UCPD and ResLife leadership during and after an active threat event.

Understanding the active threat landscape

An active threat is any situation in which an individual is actively attempting to inflict harm on people in a populated area. Most active threat events on college campuses are over within minutes, often before law enforcement arrives. That timeline is the reason this training exists.

What an active threat can include
  • An armed individual on campus or near a residence hall.
  • A credible threat communicated through written, verbal, or electronic channels.
  • A weapons-related incident in or near a residential community.
  • A barricade or hostage situation.
What it is not
  • A loud, distant noise that has not been confirmed as a threat.
  • A rumor circulating on social media without verification.
  • A non-weapon altercation, no matter how disruptive.

The distinction matters because the response differs. A confirmed active threat triggers the lockdown protocol. An unconfirmed report triggers verification and elevated awareness, not panic.

Your job is not to investigate.Your job is to act on credible information, follow protocol, and protect residents in your reach.

Run, hide, fight

Run, Hide, Fight is the federally adopted response model for active threat situations. Sequence matters. Use the option available to you.

Run 01

Evacuate if a safe path exists.

  • Move quickly and quietly toward an escape route away from the threat.
  • Leave belongings behind.
  • Help others escape if possible, but do not delay your own movement.
  • Do not stop to engage with responders until you are clear.
  • Once safe, call 911 and provide your location, what you saw, and the direction of the threat.
Hide 02

Take cover if escape is not possible.

  • Move to a room that locks. Lock the door. Barricade with furniture.
  • Turn off lights. Silence phones. Close blinds.
  • Move away from doors and windows.
  • Stay low and stay quiet.
  • Communicate with 911 silently if possible.
Fight 03

As an absolute last resort.

  • When your life is in immediate danger and no other option remains.
  • Commit fully.
  • Use anything available.
  • Coordinate with others around you.
Run, then hide, then fight.In that order, when each option is no longer viable.

The ResLife lockdown protocol

Residence halls have a defined lockdown protocol that overlays Run, Hide, Fight with role-specific actions for staff.

Trigger

A confirmed BruinAlert, UCPD directive, or credible immediate threat in or near the building.

Staff actions during lockdown

  • Secure your immediate area first.
  • If safe, prompt residents in your line of sight to enter rooms and lock doors.
  • Do not attempt to clear the building. That is not your role.
  • Do not open exterior doors to unverified individuals.
  • Stay in radio and phone contact with the duty chain.
  • Document time-stamps as you go: alert received, actions taken, status updates.

What residents should do

  • Stay in their room. Lock the door. Stay away from windows.
  • Silence phones. Stay off social media speculation.
  • Wait for an official BruinAlert all-clear.

What ends a lockdown

  • Only an official UCPD or BruinAlert all-clear.
  • Not a quiet hallway. Not the absence of police presence. Not the passage of time.

Communication during an active threat

Communication during an active threat is highly structured. Follow the structure.

Information sources you trust
  • BruinAlert
  • UCPD
  • Your response chain (in Residential Education: RD → AD → leadership)
Sources you do not act on without verification
  • Social media posts
  • Text-message rumors
  • Resident reports of "they heard" or "someone said"
What you communicate
  • Verified information through your response chain.
  • Status of your assigned area to your supervisor.
  • Specific resident concerns when escalation is required.
What you do not communicate
  • Speculation.
  • Information to families directly. Strategic Communications and OEM lead family-facing communications during active threats.
  • Information to media. Refer media contacts to UCLA Strategic Communications.
A staff member who passes along an unverified rumor can become the source of a secondary crisis.Verify before you transmit.

After the threat

When the threat is over, the work continues.

Immediate post-event actions

  • Wait for an official all-clear before changing your posture.
  • Account for residents in your area according to roll-call procedures.
  • Document everything: what you saw, what you did, what time, what was communicated.
  • Connect with your supervisor and supervision chain.

Reunification

Family reunification is coordinated by Emergency Management. Staff support the process by following directives, not by initiating contact with families.

Recovery

Active threat events leave a lasting impact on staff and residents.

  • Counseling and Psychological Services and the Behavioral Intervention Team will offer support resources.
  • Resident concerns will surface in waves over the days and weeks that follow.
  • Staff debriefs will be scheduled.
  • Use the staff support resources made available to you. Do not white-knuckle through it.
Closing synthesis

Train now so you can act then

Active threat is the lowest-probability, highest-stakes scenario in this curriculum. It is also the one that demands the most from your training.

Know the difference between perceived and confirmed. Know Run, Hide, Fight cold. Know your lockdown role. Trust the verified chain.

You will not be the one who ends the threat. You can be the one whose preparation kept the people in your reach safe, and whose steadiness shaped how the community recovered.
