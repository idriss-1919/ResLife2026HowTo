Why ResLife responds

Why ResLife responds

Behind every one of those numbers is a resident: a person facing a challenge or a crisis, who needed support.

Response is not theoretical.It is active. It is human.

The core purpose

ResLife responds to guarantee no resident is ever without support, no incident goes unaddressed, and no incident is left without an action plan.

Response is the responsibility of every ResLife staff member, student or professional, on duty or off, whenever we encounter a situation within our residential communities.

The three guarantees

Someone is always reachable

01

Residents, staff, and emergency responders always have a named point of contact.

Someone always has authority to act

02

Every ResLife staff member carries the authorization to assess, stabilize, support, document, and escalate.

Someone always shows up with care

03

Response transforms policy into presence.

Who we serve

Our response serves five constituencies, each with a different stake in how we show up.

  1. Residents navigating crisis, conflict, illness, or their first time away from home.
  2. Fellow staff who need backup or authorization in the moment.
  3. Families and support systems seeking reassurance, while maintaining confidentiality.
  4. Emergency services needing a knowledgeable campus contact.
  5. The institution itself, whose legal, ethical, and reputational obligations depend on structured response.

The cost of absence

When response fails, the consequences are real:

  • Incidents escalate.
  • Residents feel unsupported.
  • Staff operate without structure.
  • The institution faces legal and reputational exposure.
This work carries responsibility because it carries impact.

The philosophy of responding

Mindset over mechanics

Knowing what to do when responding matters. But how you show up while doing it matters just as much.

Two staff members can respond to the same incident, and create completely different outcomes, based on their mindset.

The difference is philosophy.Effective responders follow principles.

The five principles of effective response

Presence over performance

01

You are not performing authority. You are genuinely available to offer support and care.

Curiosity before judgment

02

Seek to understand before deciding what to do.

Calmness as a professional tool

03

Your ability to manage your emotions helps regulate the environment.

Accountability without rigidity

04

Upholding standards while recognizing human complexity.

Completing the loop

05

Response does not end when the incident ends. Documentation, communication, and follow-up are not optional, they are part of the response.

You are not alone

Response is a system, not a solo act. There are escalation protocols, campus partners, and your supervisor or on-call leadership.

You are not expected to always have the answer. The expectation is that you always take responsible action.

Knowing when to call for help shows strength and professionalism.

Why this work matters

Most people choose this work because they want to make a difference. Responding is not separate from that purpose.

The hallway conversation. The call you answer. The resident who just needed someone to talk to in the moment.

Those are not interruptions to your job.They are the job.

Expectations when you encounter a situation

Whether you are on a duty schedule or going about your regular workday, you may encounter situations that require a response. Any ResLife staff member, student or professional, on or off the schedule, is expected to respond when they encounter an incident in our residential communities.

When that moment comes, your role is to stabilize, support, and connect.

Stabilize

01

Stabilize the immediate environment. Ensure safety. Reduce risk.

Support

02

Support the people in front of you with care, presence, and clarity.

Connect

03

Connect the situation to the duty system, by contacting the staff on duty, escalating to your supervisor or on-call leadership, or contacting UCPD or 911 when warranted.

Important You do not need to take ownership of the entire incident. You do need to make sure it does not go unaddressed because you happened to be off the schedule.
The duty system is the floor of our response coverage, not the ceiling.Every ResLife staff member is part of the safety net.
For RESED staff Residential Education-specific expectations while on the duty schedule (rounds, communication devices, documentation deadlines, on-call standards) are covered in RESED Unit Core training.
The bottom line

The responsibility and the structure

Response is not simply about answering a phone. It is a system of protection. It is a structure of accountability. It is a visible promise to residents that someone is steady when things are not.

When you respond, you represent more than yourself. You represent your team. You represent the department. You represent the institution's commitment to care and safety.

And you are not alone in that responsibility.

R 102

The BOARDS Method

The discipline behind a calm, capable response.

What each letter of BOARDS promises about the quality of a response, why a structured method outperforms improvisation, and how the six steps reinforce one another in the moment and across cycles.

Why a method, not improvisation

When a resident is in crisis, when an alarm sounds, when a roommate conflict turns into something more — the responder does not have time to invent a process from scratch. They need a discipline they have already practiced, a sequence they trust, and a shared language with the rest of the team.

BOARDS is that discipline. Six letters. Six promises. One method that ties them together.

The bottom line

BOARDS is not a checklist. It is a discipline.

A checklist tells you what to do. A discipline shapes who you are when you do it. BOARDS works because each step earns the right to the next — skipping any one of them weakens the whole.

B  ·  Breathe

"A regulated responder makes the right call."

B · Stress regulation as the precondition for action
What it promises Why it precedes the next step What happens without it
A responder bringing a regulated nervous system to whatever happens next. You cannot observe accurately, decide soundly, or communicate clearly while flooded with stress chemistry. Breathe sets the body up for Observe. Reaction replaces response. Tunnel vision narrows what you can see. Decisions are emotional, not strategic. The rest of BOARDS sits on a shaky foundation.

O  ·  Observe

"See clearly before you act."

O · Situational awareness before commitment
What it promises Why it precedes the next step What happens without it
An accurate read of the situation — scope, scale, people, hazards, resources — before the responder commits to anything. The action you choose is only as good as the picture you see. Observe makes Act intentional. First-impression bias drives the call. The wrong threat gets prioritized. The actual hazard gets missed. Bystanders are mistaken for participants and vice versa.

A  ·  Act

"Calm voice. Clear protocol. Right call."

A · Deliberate response, not improvisation
What it promises Why it precedes the next step What happens without it
A protocol-aligned response that prioritizes by severity and safety. Action only works if it preserves the option to escalate or call in resources. Act makes Refer possible. Reactive moves close down options. Improvised responses cannot be replicated by the next responder. Patterns of practice cannot be tested or improved.

R  ·  Refer

"You are part of a network. Use it."

R · Resource utilization, not solo heroism
What it promises Why it precedes the next step What happens without it
The responder is part of a network — not the only person on the case. Documentation is meaningful only when it captures who else was involved and what they did. Refer makes Document complete. One person carries weight that should be distributed. Critical resources go untapped. The institution cannot build a coordinated response. Burnout becomes the predictable outcome.

D  ·  Document

"If it isn't documented, it didn't happen."

D · Records that survive the moment
What it promises Why it precedes the next step What happens without it
A record that captures what happened — accurately, objectively, in time — so it survives the moment. A debrief is only useful if there is a reliable account of what happened. Document makes Synthesize trustworthy. Memories drift. Patterns aren't visible to leadership or partners. Compliance fails. The next staff member who needs to understand this incident has nothing to learn from.

S  ·  Synthesize

"An incident isn't finished until it has been processed."

S · Closure that feeds the next cycle
What it promises Why it precedes the next step What happens without it
The lessons of this incident — practical, emotional, structural — enter the next one. Today's debrief is tomorrow's first Breathe, calmer and sharper. Synthesize makes the next BOARDS cycle better. The same mistakes repeat. Vicarious trauma accumulates in the responder's body and gets carried into the next call. Staff don't get better at this work; they just get tired.

A sequence & a cycle

BOARDS is not a one-way street. It is both a sequence (in the moment) and a cycle (over time).

In the moment — a sequence

→

B → O → A → R → D → S

Each step in BOARDS prepares the responder for the next. Breathe before you Observe. Observe before you Act. Act so that you can Refer. Refer so that you can Document. Document so that you can Synthesize. The order matters because each step earns the right to the one that follows.

Over time — a cycle

↻

S → B (next call)

Synthesize is not the end. It is the on-ramp to the next response. What the responder learns, processes, and integrates today shapes the regulation, the awareness, and the judgment they bring to the next call. Done well, BOARDS makes a responder better. Done poorly, the same call costs more every time.

The bottom line

BOARDS is not a checklist. It is a discipline.

Knowing it doesn't make a staff member ready for crisis. Practicing it does. Each step is a promise about the quality of the response — and a debt the responder pays to the resident on the other side of the door.

Bring the body. See clearly. Move with intention. Use the network. Leave a record. Carry the lesson forward.

R 103

Legal & Compliance Framework

The frameworks behind why we document — what each law protects, who it serves, and the principle behind every interaction.

Five federal and state laws set the floor for how Residential Life operates. This module is the framework. Knowing it shapes every decision a staff member makes.

Five laws, one ethic

Residential Life operates inside a federal and state legal architecture that protects every person living in or visiting our communities. The five laws below shape what we record, what we share, who we report to, and how we respond when something has gone wrong. Every staff member — on duty or not, in resident-facing roles or in operations — works inside this framework.

The bottom line

These laws aren't bureaucracy. They're the floor under every interaction.

The framework exists so that residents know what to expect from us, partners know how we'll work with them, and staff know which actions are mandatory, which are protected, and which are out of bounds.

CANRA — Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act

"When a child is at risk, the law requires you to act, not deliberate."

CANRA · California Penal Code §§ 11164–11174.3
What it protects Who it serves Why it matters for residents
Children under 18 from physical, sexual, and emotional abuse, neglect, exploitation, and unlawful corporal punishment. Any child the staff member encounters in the course of their work — including students under 18 enrolled at UCLA, minor visitors, and the children of resident families. Some of our residents are minors. Some adult residents have minor children at home. Family members visit. CANRA makes us mandated reporters when we encounter, observe, or learn of suspected abuse — the threshold for reporting is reasonable suspicion, not certainty.

FERPA — Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act

"A student's information is theirs. Trust is the currency of access to it."

FERPA · 20 U.S.C. § 1232g
What it protects Who it serves Why it matters for residents
The privacy of student education records — grades, conduct history, disability accommodations, residency and any personally identifiable information held by an institution receiving federal funds. Every enrolled student, regardless of age. Parents, partners, and family do not have automatic access to a student's records — even if they pay tuition. Residents tell us things they wouldn't tell their parents. They consent to share information with us, not with whoever calls asking about them. FERPA is the legal expression of that trust: information shared for one purpose is not transferable to another.

The Clery Act

"Sunlight is the safety mechanism — campuses disclose so communities can choose."

Jeanne Clery Disclosure Act · 20 U.S.C. § 1092(f)
What it protects Who it serves Why it matters for residents
The right of prospective and current students, employees, and the public to know about crime on, adjacent to, and within campus through public crime logs and an annual security report. Anyone making a decision about being on campus — a prospective applicant, a current resident weighing safety, a parent assessing risk, a researcher studying patterns, a journalist holding institutions accountable. Without Clery, a campus could quietly sit on a pattern of incidents that shapes whether residents are actually safe. Clery requires that pattern to be visible. The reports we write at the floor level become the data points that hold the institution accountable.

Title IX — Sex-Based Discrimination & Harassment

"Equal access to education means freedom from sex-based harm."

Title IX of the Education Amendments of 1972 · 20 U.S.C. § 1681
What it protects Who it serves Why it matters for residents
The right of every person at a federally funded education program to participate fully — free of sex-based discrimination, sexual harassment, sexual violence, and retaliation for reporting. Every resident, every student, every staff member, every visitor, regardless of gender identity, sexual orientation, or any other axis. Title IX protects all sexes and genders. Residence halls and apartments are where sexual misconduct most often happens on a college campus. Title IX is the framework that says the institution cannot look away — and that no staff member's role permits them to do so either. As Responsible Employees, we share what we learn so the institution can offer the right support.

Title VI — Bias & Discrimination

"Belonging is a civil right. Bias is its violation."

Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 · 42 U.S.C. § 2000d
What it protects Who it serves Why it matters for residents
The right to access an educational program free from discrimination, harassment, profiling, or hostile conduct based on race, color, ethnicity, national origin, religion, citizenship status, or related characteristics. Every member of the community — disproportionately, residents from historically marginalized backgrounds whose access to belonging is most at risk when bias goes unaddressed. A bias incident in a residential community doesn't just hurt the resident it targets. It shapes whether the entire floor, building, or community feels like a place they belong. Title VI is the legal floor under the work that ResLife calls Inclusive Excellence — and the Civil Rights Office is the partner that takes formal cases to resolution.

How the five frameworks fit together

Each law protects something specific. Read together, they describe the floor of safety, dignity, and access that every member of our community is owed.

Safety floor

01

CANRA + Clery

Children are protected from harm; campuses must disclose patterns of harm so the broader community can act on what they show.

Privacy floor

02

FERPA

A student's record is theirs to control. Information given to us for one purpose does not transfer to others without their consent.

Equal-access floor

03

Title IX

No one's access to an education is conditioned on tolerating sex-based harm. The institution is accountable for ensuring it.

Belonging floor

04

Title VI

Race, ethnicity, religion, national origin, and related identities are not grounds for exclusion or hostility in any educational program.

The bottom line

Five laws. One ethic.

Every resident is owed the protections each of these guarantees — whether or not the staff member ever opens an Incident Report, whether or not the partner office is ever called, whether or not the case ever reaches a hearing.

The framework isn't a checklist for incidents. It's the standard for the daily work.

Who responds to incidents?

Clarify each responder's role within the layered response structure (first responder, supervisor escalation, leadership engagement) and map the campus and community partners who support incident response. Eliminate ambiguity in authority, scope, and partner referrals.

  2. Distinguish between student staff, professional staff, and campus partner roles.

The layered response structure

Response is not a solo act. It is a layered response system, and every incident moves through defined levels of authority.

The goal is not independence.The goal is structured response.
What it ensures
  • Immediate response
  • Supervision and support
  • Escalation when necessary
  • Departmental and institutional oversight
What understanding it prevents
  • Overstepping authority
  • Delayed escalation
  • Gaps in supervision
  • Institutional risk
Why it matters Each level carries defined authority and responsibility. Knowing where you sit within that structure protects you, the resident, the department, and the institution.

Response is everyone's responsibility

Duty is a structured system, but incident response is not limited to the people on the schedule. Any ResLife staff member, student or professional, on duty or off, is expected to respond when they encounter an incident in our residential communities.

This includes:

  • The RA who walks past a noise violation on their way back from class.
  • The RD who passes through a community where they are not assigned.
  • The professional staff member who hears a fire alarm in their office building.
  • The graduate intern who witnesses a conflict in a hallway.
  • The student leader who is present when a peer is in distress.

If you encounter a situation, your responsibility is to stabilize, support, and connect: ensure immediate safety, provide care in the moment, and connect the situation to the duty system by contacting the staff on duty or, when needed, escalating directly to your Supervisor on Duty (SOD), UCPD, or 911.

You do not need to take ownership of the entire incident. You do need to make sure it does not go unaddressed because you happened to be off the schedule.

The duty system is the floor of our response coverage, not the ceiling.Every staff member is part of the safety net.

The three tiers of response

Every ResLife unit operates within the same three-tier response structure, even when the specific role titles, schedules, and protocols vary by unit. Knowing the tiers makes it possible for any staff member to act, escalate, and connect across the department.

First responder

Tier 01

The staff member closest to the situation. They make initial contact, assess what is happening, take immediate action within their scope, and document.

Responsibilities
  • Initial assessment
  • On-site response within scope
  • Stabilization and care
  • Documentation
  • Escalation when the situation exceeds first-responder scope

Not expected to have every answer. Expected to take responsible action and escalate when the situation calls for additional support or oversight.

Supervisor escalation

Tier 02

The professional staff member who provides consultation, supervision, and on-site response when the first responder needs support or the incident exceeds first-responder scope.

Engaged when
  • The incident exceeds first-responder scope
  • There is ambiguity in authority or next steps
  • Institutional reporting may be triggered
  • Coordination with other partners or units is needed

Leadership engagement

Tier 03

Senior leadership coordinates major crisis response, engages cross-unit and cross-departmental partners, and connects with the University Crisis Response Team when necessary.

Activated for
  • High-risk incidents
  • Widespread disruption or significant institutional impact
  • Major safety and life events
  • Situations requiring cross-departmental or executive-level coordination
Knowing your unit's response chain Every unit's named response chain (who fills Tier 1, Tier 2, and Tier 3 in your day-to-day work) is covered in your Unit Core training. In Residential Education, the response chain runs Duty RA / ARA → Duty RD → Duty AD → Department Leadership. Other units structure and name their own chains. Know yours cold.

The bottom line

Structure creates safety

Response is layered for a reason. Clear roles prevent confusion. Defined authority prevents overreach. Partnership prevents isolation.

Response is not about handling everything. It is about knowing who handles what, and when to activate them.

Scheduling principles

Effective duty scheduling is critical to consistent and reliable coverage. A well-planned schedule ensures fair and equitable rotation, flexibility, and clear communication.

Weekday vs. weekend Sunday through Wednesday shifts count as weekday shifts. Thursday through Saturday shifts count as weekend shifts. Schedulers aim to distribute shift types — weekdays, weekends, holiday/special event, and Duty for Dollars — as evenly as possible.

Staff indicate shift preferences through a survey or scheduling software. Preferences are taken into account, but operational requirements take priority. Schedules are shared at least two weeks before the quarter starts, and staff are encouraged to provide feedback on the scheduling process.

Duty switches

After the duty schedule is established, when a duty switch is needed, it is the responsibility of the individual on duty (RA, RD, or AD) to secure coverage for their shift.

  1. Contact each member of your duty rotation individually to request coverage.
  2. If no one can cover the shift, contact your supervisor or duty scheduler for further assistance.
  3. For staff vacancies, schedulers will ask staff with fewer accumulated duty days to cover the shift.
  4. For day-of RD or AD coverage, ResLife can add notes to the duty sheet via the "Duty Notes" feature for partial-day coverage. Contact the Duty AD to notify them and add the note.
Departing staff Staff members leaving the role should not be making duty trades for days after their leave date. If this occurs, the staff member they traded with may be assigned the day back.
The bottom line

A fair, predictable rotation makes coverage real.

Schedules are shared two weeks early so staff can plan around them. Holidays and special events distribute evenly across the team. Duty for Dollars is voluntary and paid. Switches are owned by the on-duty staff member, with supervisor backup.

If you can't cover, find coverage. If you can't find coverage, escalate. The schedule only works when everyone owns their day.

Breathe

The first step in BOARDS. Calm the mind and body when the nervous system is activated.

  2. Apply the 4·4·6 deep-breathing cycle to slow the body and clear the mind in the moment.
  3. Use the 5-4-3-2-1 grounding technique to anchor attention back to the present.

Method lens BOARDS as a method lives in R 102: The BOARDS Method. R 102 covers what each letter of BOARDS promises about quality of response and why a structured method outperforms improvisation. This module is the operational guide for Breathe on duty.

Why we breathe first

In a crisis, the first move isn't action — it's regulation. The rest of the response depends on a calm nervous system. You can't observe accurately, decide soundly, or communicate clearly when you're flooded with stress chemistry.

Calm the body

01

Slow the activated nervous system back toward homeostasis. Lower heart rate, ease tension, restore steady breath.

Clear the mind

02

Reduce cognitive load so you can think clearly and make sound decisions instead of reacting on autopilot.

Stay close to homeostasis

03

The closer you stay to baseline, the more accurate your read of the situation will be — and the better your call.

Respond, don't react

04

A regulated responder chooses; a dysregulated one is chosen for. Breathe is what makes choice possible.

Foundational

Breathe is not a luxury. It is the precondition for everything else in BOARDS.

Deep breathing

A simple cycle of slow inhalation, hold, and longer exhalation activates the parasympathetic nervous system and brings the body back toward calm.

5-step cycle 4 · 4 · 6 breath
Step 01
Settle
Sit or stand comfortably.
Step 02 · 4 ct
Inhale through nose
Breathe in deeply through your nose for a count of four.
Step 03 · 4 ct
Hold
Hold the breath for a count of four.
Step 04 · 6 ct
Exhale through mouth
Exhale slowly through your mouth for a count of six.
Step 05
Repeat
Continue the cycle for several minutes.
Why the longer exhale Exhalation is the brake pedal. A longer exhale relative to the inhale signals safety to the nervous system. Even one cycle slows the heart rate and softens the stress response. Make the exhale count.

Grounding techniques

When attention is racing, anchor it back to your senses. The 5-4-3-2-1 method walks the mind back into the body.

CountPractice
5 thingsYou can see

Naming each one — silently or out loud — pulls awareness from the spiral and back to the present.

Body scan

Tension hides in the body before the mind catches it. A short scan finds the tight places and releases them.

Step 01
Settle & breathe
Close your eyes and take a few deep breaths.
Step 02
Scan head to toe
Slowly scan your body from head to toe, noticing any areas of tension.
Step 03
Release on the exhale
As you identify tension, visualize it melting away with each exhale.
Common spots

Forehead, jaw, shoulders, hands, stomach.

Most stress lives in five places. Scan them in order. Soften each one. Move on. The whole practice can take 90 seconds before a hard conversation or shift handoff.

Tips for daily practice

Practice when calm

01

Build resilience by practicing these exercises regularly. The skill you can use in a crisis is the skill you've already rehearsed.

Reach for it under pressure

02

Use these techniques in the moment — before a hard call, before knocking on a difficult door, before walking into the unknown.

The bottom line

A regulated responder makes the right call.

You can't out-think a flooded nervous system. You can only outpace it by getting back to baseline first. Take the four-count breath. Run the five-sense scan. Soften the jaw and shoulders.

Calm the body. Clear the mind. Then begin.

What Observe is for

Accurately assessing a situation is what turns a duty staffer from someone who responded into someone who responded well. Observe slows you down long enough to see what is actually happening — the scope, the people, the hazards, the resources — before you commit to action.

Assess scope & scale

01

Understand the size and severity of the incident. One floor or one building? One person or several? Minor or major?

Scan with all senses

02

Sight, sound, smell — even touch. The full picture is rarely captured by any single sense alone.

Personal safety first

03

Before you act for anyone else, confirm you are safe. If not, escalate to backup, SOD, or emergency services.

Identify source & resources

04

What started this? Who is involved and what is their condition? What's available — both as resource and as hazard?

Observation techniques

360-degree scan

01

Look around in all directions. Use all your senses to detect changes in the environment.

  • Don't rely on what's directly in front of you
  • What's behind, above, and beside you matters too
  • Note exits, lit hallways, and visible faces

Identify hazards

02

Look for anything that might pose danger to yourself or others.

  • Identify potential resources and safe areas
  • Find exits and allies before you need them
  • Note environmental risks: smoke, spills, broken glass

Assessment tools

Tool 01 People observation

Observe the behavior and condition of those involved. Note any signs of distress, injury, or unusual behavior — both verbal and nonverbal.

What to look for

Behavior, body, and breath.

Pace and content of speech · posture and movement · eye contact and facial expression · breathing pattern · visible injuries or signs of substance use · response to questions or instructions · who is calm, who is escalating, and what each person seems to need.

Tool 02 SWOT analysis

A fast mental framework for sizing up a situation under pressure.

LetterStands forQuestion
SStrengthsWhat resources and support are available? Backup staff, partners on the way, a calm resident, a clear hallway.
WWeaknessesWhat are the limitations or risks? Communication issues, missing equipment, time pressure, your own state.
OOpportunitiesWhat can be done to improve the situation? An open door, a willing witness, a calmer space available nearby.
TThreatsWhat are the immediate dangers? Fire, weapons, escalating violence, medical urgency, environmental hazards.

Tips for staying observant

  • Personal safety first. Escalate to backup, SOD, or emergency services if needed. You can't help anyone if you become a second casualty.
  • Continuously reassess. A situation evolves. The picture you took at minute one is not the picture at minute five.
  • Observe before assuming. The first explanation that comes to mind is rarely the right one. Wait for the data to tell you.
  • Trust your instincts. If something feels off, that feeling is information. Don't override it without checking what triggered it.
The bottom line

See clearly. Then act.

Scan with all your senses. Read the people. Run a fast SWOT. Confirm you're safe. Identify the source, the hazards, and the resources.

The action you take is only as good as the observation it's built on. Take the extra ten seconds. They almost always pay off.

What is an Incident Report?

An Incident Report (IR) is a detailed, chronological record of an event — capturing the circumstances before, during, and after an incident. These reports are legal documents, essential for record keeping, compliance, policy enforcement, student support, and follow-up.

Deadline

All IRs should be submitted in Advocate by 10 AM following the incident.

Or ASAP when urgent and directed. Staff are expected to document all incidents both on and off duty.

  • Mental health
  • Injury / medical

  • Facilities / maintenance concerns

How to write an IR

When did it happen?

01

Begin with the date and time. Write events chronologically with accurate timestamps. Include the time the incident ended.

Where did it happen?

02

Include the location of the concern — building, floor, room number, or specific area.

Who was involved?

03

All parties and witnesses with names, roles, and affiliations. Use full names first, then first names. For residents, include UID and residential community on first mention.

What happened?

04

Use objective language to document what you see, hear, and smell. Describe facts and observable behaviors. Include direct quotes when possible.

Objective vs. opinionated

Show, don't conclude.

"Resident Joe repeatedly interrupted RA Cate and closed the door in their face" — good. "Resident Joe was rude" — avoid.

The first describes behavior; the second is a judgment that doesn't belong in a legal document.

Format How to format the IR
  • Write using third person (they / he / she / it) and past tense.
  • Break up paragraphs for easier reading.
  • Proofread to ensure it's free of grammar or spelling errors.
  • Attach relevant documents (photos, text messages, emails) with identifiable labels. Reference all attached items within the body of your IR.
  • Attachments should reflect what's written — not introduce new information.
  • Delete any notes / photos you've kept once the IR is submitted.

Framework lens The framework — what each of these five laws protects, who it serves, and why it matters — lives in R 103: Legal & Compliance Framework. What follows is the operational guide for how each law shapes what we write and what we share.
Law 01 CANRA — Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act
What it coversHow to respond
Physical injury or death of a child (under 18) by other than accidental means. Includes willful harm, sexual abuse, endangerment, unlawful corporal punishment. Support the impacted individual and offer resources. Call up to your SOD to consult. With approval, call UCPD (310-825-1491). Complete the Suspected Child Abuse Report (SCAR, Form SS-8572) and submit to UCPD within 36 hours. Make an internal report to UC Compliance (800-403-4733) or online.
Law 02 FERPA — Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act
What it coversHow to respond
Federal law protecting the privacy of student education records. Personally identifiable information may not be disclosed without student written consent. Inquire about the concern. Validate emotions. Confirm staff care about wellbeing. Explain you're limited in what you can share due to FERPA. Encourage the individual to ask their student to connect with staff. Provide resources. Escalate if persistent. Never discuss work outside of work.
Law 03 CLERY — Jane Clery Act
What it coversHow to respond
Requires colleges to disclose crime statistics on, adjacent to, or within campus — in the public crime log and the school's Clery Report. When addressing illegal activity (underage drinking, drug use), verify and document specifically — e.g., "they were holding a bottle of tequila" or "in possession of marijuana paraphernalia."
Law 04 Title IX
What it coversHow to respond
Prohibits sex / gender discrimination in any education program receiving federal funding. The Title IX Office receives reports of gender discrimination, sexual harassment, and sexual violence. Inform resident of "responsible employee" status and confidentiality limits. Offer to listen if comfortable (don't ask prying questions). Share resources: CAPS / CARE, Santa Monica Rape Treatment Center, Title IX, UCPD. Stick to wellbeing. Document. Consult SOD. Conduct will notify Title IX.
Law 05 Title VI — Bias
What it coversHow to respond
UCLA's Principles of Community address discrimination, harassment, profiling, or any conduct that harms individuals based on race, color, ethnicity, gender, age, disability, religion, political preference, sexual orientation, gender identity, citizenship, or national origin. Support the impacted resident. Offer resources. Provide the Civil Rights Office (CRO) and Dean of Students reporting links. Collect direct quotes and documentation / images. Escalate immediately to Duty AD if threatening language or behavior is involved. Conduct will issue a bias notification.

Post-incident analysis & support

An incident doesn't end when the call wraps. The Synthesize step closes the loop: a structured review of what happened, who needs follow-up, and how the response could be better next time.

Check in with responders

01

Reflect on the response. Identify what went well and what needs improvement. Ask each responder how they're holding up — not just how the incident resolved.

Conduct a low-impact debrief

02

Hold a debriefing session with all involved staff to discuss the response, emotional impact, and lessons learned. Keep it low-pressure — the goal is reflection, not performance review.

Debrief only with involved staff

03

Confidentiality is part of the work. Debrief with the people who responded and / or your supervisor. Do not share details with uninvolved staff or outside the work setting.

Find additional support

04

Student staff: SOD, supervisor, ResLife MHU, and / or CAPS. Professional staff: supervisor, ResLife MHU, and / or Faculty & Staff Counseling.

Debrief as ongoing practice
